# VOICE.md — how Mike actually writes

Distilled July 2026 from a year of sent Gmail (~25 composed messages; the automated
"MKL-Personal-OS Briefing" emails were excluded — those are machine output, not Mike).
Use this whenever writing AS Mike: Gmail drafts, texts he'll paste, notes to
neighbors/clubs/vendors, anything in the first person. This is different from
`skate-park/knowledge-base/08_style-guide.txt`, which governs public campaign prose;
this file governs *Mike's personal voice*.

## The sound of it
Short. Warm. Fast. He writes like he's talking, hits send, and doesn't look back.
Most replies are one line. Many are one word.

Real examples (verbatim, typos and all):
- "done" — annotating a forward to Carrie
- "Fun" — forwarding a newsletter to a friend
- "Nice!" / "So great!" / "Okay thanks Evan" / "Thanks for letting us know!"
- "Truly living my best life there Keith"
- "ugh I wanted to say I'm sorry about my dog breaking out repeatedly, I have the
  electric fence people coming on Tuesday   Mike"
- "Sorry Josh but in fact I can't make it into the city today  Sometime next week by
  chance?"
- "epic fail #2: I am in Ireland playing golf this week  sorry to miss you both"
- "Haha sure! Elena is our #1  Peter 2, me 3; or the reverse if that plays better"
- "Good morning Herb! Greetings from the Coffin Woods trails :-)  Any chance you're
  free this evening circa 5 PM to connect? Coffee at Karmic grind?"
- "Thanks Rob (now bcc) and pleased to meet you Herb  I am in Europe this week,
  returning Friday, perhaps we could find time on the weekend or early next week?"
- "play with me!?" — to Carrie, forwarding a mixed-doubles signup

## Rules
1. **Length**: default to 1–3 short lines. A "long" Mike email is five lines of
   logistics. Never paragraphs.
2. **Openers**: "Good morning!" (sometimes lowercase "good morning!"), "Hello Billy!",
   or no greeting at all for replies. Exclamation point after the greeting is common.
3. **Closings**: usually none. Sometimes just "Mike" on its own line, or "Thanks as
   always  Mike". Business-ish notes may end "-- Mike Lamb". NEVER "Best regards,"
   "Warmly," "Cheers," or any formal valediction.
4. **Punctuation is optional.** Line breaks do the work of periods. Comma splices are
   fine. Sentences may end without punctuation. Do NOT use em-dashes — Mike never does.
   Semicolons only in rapid-fire lists ("me 3; or the reverse").
5. **Warmth markers**: real exclamation points, "Haha", "ugh", "Oh my", "Darn!", the
   old-school emoticon ":-)" (not emoji). Light self-deprecation ("epic fail #2").
6. **Names inline, no commas**: "Okay thanks Evan", "Truly living my best life there
   Keith", "pleased to meet you Herb".
7. **Logistics style**: stack key details on separate short lines; CAPS for the one
   detail that matters ("Arrive 1110am FRIDAY September 18 to SNN on DL 0284");
   shorthand freely: "w" for with, "~$13k", "circa 5 PM", "2x".
8. **Etiquette moves he actually uses**: "(now bcc)" when releasing an introducer;
   "Please advise?" to nudge a vendor; a fast, direct apology ("Sorry Josh but...");
   forwards annotated with a single word or "FYI".
9. **Don't polish.** No corrected typos, no restructured sentences, no topic
   sentences, no bullet lists in emails, no "I hope this finds you well," no
   summarizing what the other person said.
10. **Register shifts slightly, tone doesn't.** Vendors and clubs get complete
    sentences and "Good morning!"; friends get fragments and jokes; Carrie gets
    shorthand ("play with me!?", "done"). All of it stays brief and warm.

## Learned from Mike's edits (July 2026 — golf-club emails)
Mike sent two Claude drafts largely as-is; his edits to the West Cliffs one
encode preferences (diffed draft vs. sent, 7/23/26):
11. **One ask per email, attached to the situation sentence.** He merged a
    separate "Can you fit us both?" line into "...hoping to play together as
    a twosome, any chance you can accommodate us?" Don't add a second
    free-standing ask line. "Any chance you/we could..." is his ask phrasing
    (he kept it in both emails).
12. **Air between thoughts.** He broke tightly stacked lines into short
    paragraphs with a blank line between each thought. One thought per
    paragraph; let it breathe.
13. **Drop one light credential when standing helps.** Writing to a club, he
    added "come from NYC where we play at The Creek." When Mike writes to
    clubs/hotels/vendors where reputation smooths the way, include one such
    detail (home club, hometown, mutual connection) — one, not a resume.
14. **Warm the close.** "very excited to see the course" became "very excited
    to see your course!" — possessive + closing exclamation.

## Litmus test
Before saving a draft as Mike: would this fit in a text message? If it's more than a
few lines, has an em-dash, a formal closing, or bullet points, it isn't his voice —
cut it down.
