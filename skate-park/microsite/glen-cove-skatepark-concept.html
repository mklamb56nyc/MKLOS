Part One — The Opportunity
01

Our kids need this now

Every kid needs a free place to be active and belong.

Strip it down to what's universal: whatever a family earns, a kid needs a safe, free place to move their body, make friends, and belong — somewhere outdoors that's theirs, that costs nothing to walk into. Fewer and fewer kids have one, and the shortage falls hardest on the kids with the least. A skatepark fits that need almost exactly.

① Healthy

Outdoor exercise, stress relief, joy

Exercise that doesn't feel like exercise — outdoors, creative, self-paced, chosen by the kid instead of assigned by an adult. That matters, because organized sports lose most kids in their early teens; skateboarding is what catches them. Kids themselves say they show up to have fun and shake off stress, and peer-reviewed research links skating to well-being and resilience — you fall, you get up, you try the thing again.

4.2days a week the average young skater skates — steady outdoor exercise nobody had to assign, from a study of 2,000+ skaters under 26
70%of American kids quit organized sports by age 13 because it stops being fun. Skateboarding has no season and no tryout — the fun is the point
② Inclusive

A melting pot with no bench

No team to make, no season, no cut. A kid shows up at their own level, next to kids older, younger, better, and nothing like them otherwise — one of the last "third places" left, where teenagers gather in person instead of through a screen. In the largest national study of skaters ever done, the friendships built at the park cross race, class, and age in ways few sports match.

Peopleasked the best thing about their local skatepark, most skaters picked the people — not the ramps
"Safer"is how skaters of color described the skate community — they reported feeling safer inside it than in predominantly white spaces
③ Safe

A safe, well-lit place to be

A modern park sits in the open at a busy public site — lit, visible, and full: parents at the rail, little kids on scooters, teenagers coaching them. A watched, well-used park is the opposite of the empty lot people picture when they worry about "bad kids" — and the record backs it up: there are no bad kids here, just kids with nowhere to go. Give them somewhere to belong, and they use it.

90%of police officers surveyed about their town's skatepark — 102 officers across 37 states — called it an asset to the community; 85% saw complaint calls drop once it opened
↓61%drug incidents around Long Beach, CA's skatepark in the three years after it opened, the ASK Foundation reports — violent crime fell ~29%

02

And here, the need is acute — and unfair

This stretch of the North Shore isn't a little short. It's among the most starved anywhere near us.

You don't have to take our word for it. Measure it the way the field itself does, and this area doesn't just miss an ideal — it falls below the ordinary American norm. And the families with the fewest options are the ones asked to travel the farthest.

Zero

Not "below some target nobody meets" — below the actual national average. Nassau sits at roughly a fifth of the typical U.S. rate, and our own North Shore has no free public skatepark at all.

And the kids who'd use it can't get to one

The real users are kids too young to drive, so the honest test isn't how many parks sit on a map — it's whether a kid can reach one on their own. A 20-minute bike ride covers far less ground than a 20-minute drive, and by that test this area is shut out completely.

Today, every one of the ~67,000 kids in Glen Cove and the Town of Oyster Bay lives more than a 20-minute bike ride from a free skatepark — and two-thirds of them live beyond even a 20-minute drive. Reopening Bethpage helps the Town's center, but still leaves ~58,000 kids outside biking distance and ~19,000 beyond a drive. One well-placed park at City Stadium moves about 8,700 of those kids — including nine in ten Glen Cove kids — inside a bike ride, and nearly 17,000 within a short drive. Put our park and a reopened Bethpage together, and all but about 4,000 of the region's 67,000 kids are finally within a drive of one.

This has been done — and it shows us how

Towns like ours have already pulled this off.

This is not a moonshot. All over the country — some places smaller than us, some that started from a rougher spot — communities turned this same idea into free concrete parks their kids love. Twelve of them, studied closely, hand us a playbook.

The playbook, distilled

Twelve parks, eight rules. Everything the plan below proposes traces back to a row in this table.

01
Keep it free
No fees, waivers, or residency gates — ever. A gate forfeits the grants, thins the crowds, and costs the community ownership that makes a park work.

02
Kids move governments
Youth petitions and rallies created these parks, funded them, and saved them — and kids who shape a place defend it for decades. The ownership is the point.

One champion who stays
The single best predictor across every story: one person — inside or outside government — who simply never leaves the issue. Persistence compounds.

Money stacks — small unlocks big, layered over years

Endow the maintenance up front

Part Two — Imagine we actually do this
05

So — where, and how?

Not a plan we're asserting. A hypothesis worth testing.

Everything below is provisional — an informed bet, not a promise. But run the playbook those other towns wrote, and here's how it could actually come together here.

Where — and who says yes

Glen Cove can just decide

Of all the communities around here, Glen Cove is the one that can move on its own — an independent city that owns its parks, answers to its own residents, and keeps grant-writing staff in-house. The lead site: John Maccarone Memorial City Stadium — "City Stadium" to everyone in town — roughly 19 acres of city-owned recreation land, central, with parking and ballfields already there, room to add a skatepark without displacing anything, and inside the state-designated disadvantaged community the park should serve. The map above is drawn from this exact spot.

Runs rule 08 — co-investment, not just permission
The open bet: a council site-approval resolution — which is, word for word, the "written approval from the controlling agency" the national grant requires. One vote, and the biggest gate is open.
Who shapes it

The kids and the neighbors, on the record

Before any drawing is final: a public survey (Hoboken's 250 voices put a skatepark at the heart of a $16.5M waterfront park), open design sessions downtown — bilingual, co-hosted with the community groups already trusted there — and local skaters at the table with the design firm, the way Port Washington's teens sat with theirs in 2010. If the first design gets rejected, good: Long Beach's skaters rejected draft one, the city redrew it, and that redraw is why the park is loved.

Runs rule 02 — kids move governments Rule 04 — ask the community first
The open bet: the community shows up. 1,214 signatures on a dormant petition say it will.
What it costs

Start real, grow it

~$1.0–1.5M

A full neighborhood-scale park lands in that band — Brentwood's $1.25M as-built, same island, same style, sits in the middle of it. But nobody needs the whole number on day one: a smaller, complete Phase 1 opens years sooner, and a used, loved park raises the money and the votes for the next pour — Rochester opened "half done" and its crowds made Phase 2 politically easy. City-owned land removes the single biggest line item before we start.

Runs rule 07 — phase it: open, then grow
The open bet: a builder's real quote from a walk of City Stadium — the firms that built our comparison parks all work here. A band, not a number, until then.
How it's paid for

No single check — a sequence

In rough order: a five-figure design gift producing a shovel-ready plan (West Bend's $53K did exactly that — and attracted a $500K lead gift within months); a lead gift structured as a match, so every private dollar pulls public ones (Ann Arbor's $400K county challenge); the national skatepark grant (up to $25K — credibility as much as cash); county open-space and parks money; the state's municipal-parks grants (up to 90% of costs, with a share reserved for designated disadvantaged communities — ours qualifies); and legislator-directed state capital, the exact mechanism that delivered Brentwood's $700K. Layered over two or three grant cycles, the way every park in the table was.

Runs rule 05 — money stacks: small unlocks big
The open bet: which layers land, and in what order. None is committed; all of them exist and fund parks like this today.
Who keeps it

Endowed before it's asked

The single biggest reason cities hesitate isn't construction — it's the fear of forever-costs. Two facts dissolve it: a properly built concrete park needs little more than trash pickup (it's why every grant requires concrete, and why Port Washington's needed no capital work for 14 years), and a "Friends of the Skatepark" nonprofit can endow the rest — roughly $10K a year in Ann Arbor's arrangement, held at a community foundation. Long Island has a community foundation ready-made for exactly that role.

Runs rule 06 — endow the maintenance up front
The open bet: a Friends board that signs the maintenance pledge in writing — and walks into city hall with it.
The one rule

It stays free. Forever.

Free, public, city-owned — no entry fee, no waiver, no residency gate, ever. That's not just principle: it's what keeps the park eligible for every grant above, and it's the whole difference between Charleston's gated $4.8M park losing its crowds and Long Beach's free one becoming the town's living room. The moment a gate goes up, the community a park exists to build stops showing up.

Runs rule 01 — keep it free, the rule that outranks the rest
Not a bet — non-negotiable.

What happens next

It starts with a conversation.

Not a grant application. Not a groundbreaking. Not a showdown at city hall. Just conversations — with a few neighbors, a few skaters, the folks already running youth programs downtown, and, in time, the city. That's the whole first move; everything real grows out of it.

One more thing the twelve parks agree on: every single one had a champion who simply wouldn't quit — a councilman who stayed 25 years, a Friends group that outlasted four mayors, a teacher who ran bake sales for nine. That's rule 03, and it's the only rule nobody can run but us.

If any of this stirred something — if you'd skate here, help build it, help fund it, or just want to see it happen — the best next step is to talk. The people who start this now become its founders.
