01

The opportunity

A gap this big is really an opening.

Around here, kids skate in parking lots, driveways, and the fronts of stores — wherever they're tolerated, until they're not. There are skateparks on the North Shore, but the nearest ones sit more than a half-hour away in either direction — and the Town of Oyster Bay's own has been shut for years. For this whole stretch — Glen Cove, Locust Valley, and the communities beside them — there's simply nowhere close to go.

So where would you actually build one? The answer that keeps surfacing is Glen Cove — not because it's the only option, but because it's the one place around here that can move on its own. It's an independent city: it owns its parks and answers to its own residents, so it can decide to build without waiting on the Town, the County, or anyone else. That's what turns a nice idea into a doable one.

And the appetite is already proven — more than a thousand people signed on to a North Shore skatepark years ago; it just never had anyone to carry it. The want is here. The place is buildable. The door is unlocked — nobody has walked through it yet.

The need

The gap isn't just felt. It's measured — and it's unfair.

You don't have to take our word for it. By the skatepark field's own yardstick — one neighborhood park per 25,000 people — this whole area is starved. And the shortage falls hardest on the kids with the least.

03

The proof

Towns like ours have already pulled this off.

This is not a moonshot. All over the region, communities — some smaller than us, some that started from a rougher place — turned this same idea into free concrete parks their kids love.

Long Beach, NY

Our peer city

The other little city in our county built a free park by the water, designed with its own skaters. Same kind of town, same kind of government. They just did it.

Brentwood, NY

Long Island

$1.25M — built

A high-schooler's petition pulled in real state money and turned a poisoned lot into a free concrete park. Youth-led, right here on the island — and our closest read on what one actually costs.

Jersey City, NJ

NY metro

A contaminated dead-end became the neighborhood's proudest park — free, concrete, shaped start to finish by the local skaters.

Rochester, NY

The long game

Once the biggest U.S. city with no skatepark. They stacked grant on grant on gift, stayed patient, and built it anyway.

04

The field

And we wouldn't be doing it alone.

Here's the part that surprises people once they look: how many hands could be on this. None of them have been asked yet — this is simply the map of who's out there, and every one of them is reachable.

The base

Neighbors & skaters

The thousand-plus who already raised their hands, the local skate scene, and the families who'd be there every weekend.

The anchors

Community groups

Downtown nonprofits already doing right by local kids — the natural partners and the most credible local voices.

The owner

The city itself

Glen Cove owns the land, runs its own recreation department, and even keeps an in-house grant writer on staff. Real muscle, already there.

The craft

The people who build these

Specialist concrete-park firms have built free parks up and down the metro and across Long Island. This is a solved craft, not an experiment.

The money

The funders

A national grant made for exactly this, a sporting-goods foundation that funds neighborhood facilities, Long Island's own family foundations, and state and county park dollars. No one source carries it — together they can.

The champions

Local & state leaders

City, county, and state representatives — the kind of people who, in towns like Brentwood, became the ones to unlock the public money that builds parks like this.

Six kinds of allies. Dozens of doors. Zero knocked on yet.

What happens next

It starts with a conversation.

Not a grant application. Not a groundbreaking. Not a showdown at city hall. Just conversations — with a few neighbors, a few skaters, the folks already running youth programs downtown, and, in time, the city.

That's the whole first move. Everything real grows out of it. If any of this stirred something — if you'd skate here, help build it, help fund it, or just want to see it happen — the best next step is to talk.
