GLEN COVE SKATEPARK — WRITING STYLE GUIDE
Created July 2026, after the owner asked that the microsite "read less like it was
written by Claude." Distilled from the de-AI pass done that day. Applies to ALL
outward-facing prose in this project (microsite, brief excerpts that go public,
letters, grant narratives). Future sessions: read this before writing or revising
public copy, and hold new drafts to it.

====================================================================
THE TELLS — what makes copy read machine-written
====================================================================
Each of these is fine once. The tell is the pattern.

1. EM-DASH ADDICTION. More than about one dash per paragraph. Machine copy leans
   on the dash for every pivot, aside, and punchline.
2. THE REVERSAL. "It isn't X — it's Y." / "Not X. Y." One per page is a strong
   move; in every section it's a verbal tic. (The old copy had ~10.)
3. THE STINGER. Ending every paragraph on a compressed aphorism ("The ownership
   is the point." "Persistence compounds."). When everything punches, nothing
   lands. Budget: the two best per page keep their punch.
4. MATCHED LISTS. Triples and quadruples with parallel grammar ("no team, no
   bench, no season, no cut"). Real writers' lists are shorter and raggeder.
5. THE COLON SETUP. "Two facts dissolve it:" / "Strip it down to what's
   universal:" — announcing the structure before delivering it. Also "Here's the
   part that surprises people:".
6. INTENSIFIER PADDING. simply, actually, literally, exactly, radically, just.
   Cut them; the sentence almost always improves.
7. DRAMATIC FRAGMENTS. "Real muscle, already there." Fine rarely; a page of them
   reads like ad copy.
8. UNIFORM RHYTHM. Medium sentence, dash, punchline — repeat. If reading aloud
   sounds like a drumbeat, the rhythm needs breaking.
9. EDITORIALIZED NUMBERS. Every stat label carrying a moral. Let some numbers
   just be numbers; the reader can feel them.

====================================================================
THE FIXES
====================================================================
- Spend rhetoric like money: one reversal and two stingers per page, on the best
  material. Everything else gets plain declarative sentences.
- Swap dashes for commas, periods, and parentheses. Vary sentence length for
  real: some long and workmanlike, some short. It's fine for a sentence to just
  end.
- Prefer concrete subjects and verbs ("the city pays nothing") over abstraction
  ("dissolves the fiscal objection").
- Keep the facts and the warmth; lose the packaging. The goal is a sharp local
  advocate on a good day, not a keynote.
- Read-aloud test before shipping anything public.
