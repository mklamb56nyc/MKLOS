# A Public Skatepark for Glen Cove
### Strategy brief and case for support

*Re-sequenced July 2026 to lead the way we want to tell it — our kids need this now;
the gap here is acute and unfair; it has been done before and shows us how; we have
the cast to pull it off; and only then, the honest early next steps — with the
hypothesis for how it actually gets built (site, cost, funding, maintenance, sequence)
kept beneath the narrative in Part Two. Prepared as a working strategy document and the source for outward-facing
materials. Figures on comparable projects are drawn from public reporting; the DAC
designation, the access/provisioning numbers, the golf-course contrast, the current
park inventory, and the build cost should all be re-verified against the relevant
agencies, a live map check, and a builder before applying or committing.*

====================================================================
PART ONE — THE OPPORTUNITY
====================================================================

## 1. Our kids need this now more than ever

Start with the kid, not the park. The four moves below build in order: name the
problem, show why *this* is the answer, clear the old objection, then hand the
skeptic hard proof.

**1a. The problem we're solving — every kid needs a free place to be active and
belong, and fewer and fewer have one.**
Strip it to what's universal: regardless of family income, a kid needs a safe, free
place to move their body, make friends, and belong — somewhere that's *theirs*,
outdoors, that costs nothing to walk into. Fewer and fewer have it, and the shortage
falls hardest on the kids with the least access.
- The need is universal; the shortage isn't evenly shared. Lower-income kids are
  **less physically active and have less access to nearby, quality parks** than
  wealthier kids — and skateparks themselves have too often been built in affluent
  suburbs that already have everything.
- It's a belonging problem as much as an activity one. What's scarce for *all* kids
  now is unstructured, face-to-face time in a place they can claim as their own — and
  that scarcity is deepest where public investment is thinnest.
- Say it plainly: "This isn't about giving kids one more thing to do. It's about
  giving *every* kid — not just the ones whose families can pay — a free place to be
  active, make friends, and belong."

**1b. Why a skatepark is one of the best answers we have.**
It fits the deficit almost exactly.
- *Exercise that doesn't feel like exercise* — outdoors, creative, self-paced.
- *Radically inclusive* — no team to make, no bench, no season, no cut. A kid shows
  up at their own level and progresses at their own pace, next to kids older,
  younger, better, worse, and nothing like them otherwise.
- *It builds the character we say we want* — persistence, patience, composure under
  frustration. "You fall, you get up, you try the thing again. That is the whole
  sport."
- *And the research backs every word of this* — in the largest national study of
  skaters (USC's "Beyond the Board," 5,000+ surveyed), the top reasons kids skate are
  **to have fun (76%)** and **to get away from stress (62%)**, and skating builds
  friendships across race, class, and age. Planners' own reference (the Public
  Skatepark Development Guide) credits it with positive youth development, mental
  resilience, belonging, and even improved academic performance.

**1c. Retire the old caricature.**
Name it so you can kill it: the skatepark as a den of smoking, tattoos, and
delinquency. That image is decades stale.
- What it actually is: an all-ages public commons — one of the last "third places"
  left where kids gather in person instead of through a screen.
- The part that matters most is **ownership** — a public place that is theirs, that
  they help design, that they look after because it belongs to them. *The ownership
  is the point as much as the ramps are.*

**1d. For the skeptic who fears it invites trouble — the evidence runs the other way.**
Where these parks open, neighborhoods tend to get *safer*, not less safe.
- The hard number: the ASK Foundation reports that within three years of Long Beach,
  California's **Michael K. Green skatepark** opening, drug-related incidents in the
  surrounding area fell roughly **61%**, violent crime roughly **29%**, and overall
  incidents roughly **23%**. And in The Skatepark Project's national survey of law
  enforcement, nearly half of officers reported reduced youth crime after a skatepark
  opened.
- The one-line answer to the safety worry: "Give kids somewhere to belong and they
  use it." A watched, well-used public space displaces the trouble people fear.

## 2. And here, the need is acute — and unfair

Zoom out and the shortage is measurable, not rhetorical. The three moves below build
in order: the supply is short by the field's own math, the kids who'd use it can't
reach one, and the contrast next door makes it unfair.

**2a. We're starved by the field's own standard — this isn't an amenity we happen to lack.**
The Public Skatepark Development Guide (the industry reference planners use) sets a
benchmark of one neighborhood skatepark per 25,000 residents. We aren't close.
- By that standard Nassau County should have about **56** and Long Island about **117**;
  our immediate North Shore stretch should have about **3**. A firmed July-2026 census of
  free, public, outdoor parks puts the real supply at roughly **9** across all of Long
  Island — **3** in Nassau, and **none** in our North Shore stretch.
- And this isn't just falling short of an ideal nobody meets: the *national average* is
  roughly one skatepark per **100,000** people, and Nassau sits at **about a fifth** of
  that — fewer skateparks per person than the typical American community.
- Say it plainly: "By the standard the whole industry plans to, our stretch of the
  North Shore should have three skateparks. It has zero."

**2b. And the kids who'd actually use it can't reach one.**
The real users are kids who can't drive — so the honest test isn't how many parks exist
on a map, but whether a kid can reach one on their own.
- **100% of Glen Cove's residents — and 100% of the state-designated Disadvantaged
  Community — live outside a 20-minute trip to any skatepark, by bike *or* car.**
- Widen the lens: **77% of Nassau County and 97% of the Town of Oyster Bay can't even
  *bike* to one.**
- One park at the Maccarone site closes roughly **90% of that gap** for Glen Cove and
  the DAC core and brings tens of thousands more across Oyster Bay into range — and
  because Bethpage's shuttered park never reached the North Shore, none of this changes
  if it reopens.
- Say it plainly: "There isn't one skatepark a Glen Cove kid can reach on their own.
  One park here fixes that for nine in ten of them."

**2c. And the contrast next door makes it unfair — not merely short.**
The numbers understate the injustice, so make it personal. The kids who need this most
live in our downtown/south core, a New York State–designated Disadvantaged Community
confirmed at the lead site.
- The contrast should stop a reader cold: within four miles of those neighborhoods sit
  **seven private eighteen-hole golf courses** — Nassau, North Shore, Glen Head,
  Brookville, Piping Rock, The Creek, and Engineers — hundreds of gated acres — while
  the nearest *public* skatepark those kids can use is a twenty-five-minute drive away.
- A golf course is recreation for someone with a membership. A skatepark is recreation
  for a kid with a board and a bike.
- And the drive is the trap: "twenty-five minutes away" means "only reachable with a
  parent who has a car and the free time for the trip" — exactly what many of these
  families don't have.
- Say it plainly: "The kids with the least access are asked to travel the farthest for
  the simplest thing."

*Translation for a grant reviewer or a council member: this is precisely the "local
data showing the community is underserved" that funders score on, and it pairs with
the state's DAC designation to make the equity case twice over.*

## 3. This has been done — and it shows us how

None of this is uncharted. First two flagships for what's *possible* — then the working
examples we actually build from, each handing us one piece of the plan that follows.
Together they're less a bibliography than a how-to.

**3a. First, the aspiration — what a skatepark can *be*.**
- **Berry Lane Park, Jersey City NJ** — a 17-acre toxic brownfield turned into a
  lower-income neighborhood's premier park (opened 2020), with a Skatepark Project grant
  and a $500,000 county Open Space grant, designed by SITE Design Group and local skaters
  throughout. Almost our exact story — told as a win.
- **Long Beach, CA (the ASK model)** — youth at the center of the design, and
  neighborhood crime that *fell* where the park opened (the figures are in §1d). Proof a
  skatepark makes a place safer, not less safe.
- Say it plainly: "This isn't a fantasy. Cities like ours turned empty and poisoned lots
  into the best thing in the neighborhood — now picture it *here*."

**3b. Then, the building blocks — each example hands us one piece of the plan.**
- **Phase it → Ketchum, ID (Guy Coles).** A small town's *free* concrete park grown in
  phases over decades on modest city money, a skate trust fund, and local philanthropy —
  our template for a strong Phase 1 that opens, gets used, and funds the next.
- **Layer the funding → Rochester, NY (ROC City).** No single source carried it: a $3M
  state grant, utility and Skatepark Project grants, a family foundation that gave
  unrestricted money *and* matched every dollar (up to $110,000), and a "Friends of"
  nonprofit — a twelve-year haul, the honest shape of the work.
- **The neighbor proof — and the cost anchor → Brentwood, NY (Roberto Clemente Park).**
  The closest match of all: one of Long Island's working-class, majority-Latino
  communities put a free concrete skatepark on a former toxic-dumping site (conceived
  2018, opened 2023) — funded in part by the very settlement the polluters paid.
  Youth-led, twice over: when inflation stalled the approved project, two student
  petitions and a youth-board rally pushed the town to act, and their assemblyman's
  $450,000 DASNY grant closed the gap within weeks — designed by the firm we'd call
  first (SITE Design Group) and built for **$1.25 million (as built)**, our best
  real-world cost anchor.
- **A city of our exact type runs one → Long Beach, NY.** The *other* independent city in
  Nassau County, same governance as Glen Cove, running a free, unfenced concrete park
  designed through public workshops.
- **Our nearest governance-and-design comp → Manorhaven / Port Washington, NY.** A North
  Shore Nassau municipality that built a free ~10,000 sq ft concrete park with
  local-skater input (Spohn Ranch), and reopened it after the pandemic when a teenager
  petitioned.
- **Private money unlocks public — and endow the upkeep → Ann Arbor, MI.** The county
  pledged up to $400,000 in matching funds conditional on private fundraising, and the
  Friends group endowed ~$10,000/yr maintenance as a condition of the deal.
- **A lead gift — and a donor-funded design — start the avalanche → West Bend, WI
  (live, opening target 2027).** A "Friends of" nonprofit formed in 2024 drew a $53,000
  gift that paid for a shovel-ready professional design — which then attracted a
  **$500,000 anonymous lead gift**. Design money is the cheapest credibility multiplier.
- **Let the community — and its kids — design it → Hoboken, NJ & Riverside Park, NYC.**
  In Hoboken a 250-person survey (63% skaters) drove the design and helped draw $500,000
  in county open-space grants (park now under construction, opening 2027); in NYC, teens
  co-designed and physically built the city's first municipal skatepark in 1996.
- **Supervision is optional; the fee/residency trap isn't → Northport, NY (Veterans
  Skatepark).** Long Island's supervised-access model (fenced, staffed for helmets) — and
  a caution, since a fee or residency gate is exactly what disqualifies a park from the grants.

**3c. The playbook these prove — the bridge to Part Two.**
Read together, the building blocks are a checklist the working plan simply executes:
- **Keep it free** — no entry fee, waiver, or residency gate — or forfeit the grants.
  The rule that outranks all the rest. → *supervision & watch-outs.*
- **Layer and sequence the funding** — no single source; lead with private dollars to
  unlock public ones, structured as a *match*. → *the funding stack.*
- **Phase it** — a complete, open Phase 1 that funds what comes next. → *what it costs.*
- **Youth- and community-led design** — a grant requirement, and what makes the park
  theirs. → *design workshops.*
- **Endow the maintenance up front** — it turns the city's biggest fear into a yes.
  → *the maintenance answer.*
- **Win co-investment and buy-in, not just permission** — real city money and visible
  neighborhood support, secured early. → *honest watch-outs.*
- Say it plainly: "None of this is invented — it's the playbook a dozen towns already
  ran. The one rule none could break is where we start: keep it free."

**And the ceiling, as the caution — Charleston, SC (SK8 Charleston).** A $4.8M, 32,500 sq
ft county destination park — thrilling to picture — but admission + waiver + gate make it
grant-*ineligible*. Say it plainly: "We can dream that big — but the day you charge
admission, you lose the grants. Ours stays free."

## 4. We have the cast this playbook needs

The playbook in §3 asks for specific things — money layered from many sources, a
youth-led design, a builder who can phase it, an endowed maintenance plan, and a city
that co-invests. Here's the striking part: for almost every one of those ingredients, we
already have someone within reach. This isn't a coalition yet — it's the makings of one,
lined up against exactly what the plan calls for.

**4a. Someone to quarterback it — and a "Friends of" to keep it free and endow it.**
*(the playbook's "keep it free" + "endow the maintenance")*
- A family ready to quarterback the effort, and a circle of Glen Cove benefactor friends
  who could be the founding funders and the **"Friends of" board** — the vehicle that
  holds private money, endows the ~$10k/yr maintenance (the Ann Arbor move), and backs
  the park without ever gating it.
- Say it plainly: "The two questions that make a city hesitate — who runs it, who
  maintains it — we can answer on day one."

**4b. The money to layer — private, public, and philanthropic.**
*(the playbook's "layer the funding" + "structure the gift as a match")*

  **DICK'S / LISC "Game On"** ($50k–$100k at 1:1).

  money through **DASNY** — the exact path Brentwood used — with County Legislator

- **Philanthropic:** **The Skatepark Project**; the **New York Community Trust – Long

- Say it plainly: "No single check builds this — and we already have a line on all three

**4c. The people to lead a youth- and community-led design.**
*(the playbook's "youth- and community-led design" + "buy-in")*

**4e. A city that can say yes — and co-invest.**
*(the playbook's "co-investment, not just permission")*

- Say it plainly: "The people who can approve this *and* put money behind it are all
  local. We can reach every one of them."

*Two honest notes we keep in front of us. First: almost none of these people have been
asked yet — this is the cast that **could** help, not a list of supporters, and nobody
gets represented as on board until they've actually said yes. Second, on optics: since
our family lives in Locust Valley rather than Glen Cove, Glen Cove residents should be
the public face, with the family organizing behind them. (Locust Valley has a genuine
tie regardless — Morgan Park's charter serves both, and the 2020 petition named Locust
Valley as part of the same community.)*

====================================================================
PART TWO — THE HYPOTHESIS: imagine we run the playbook here
====================================================================

*This isn't a plan we're asserting — it's a hypothesis to test. Part One set up the
opportunity and the playbook that proves it's winnable; here we imagine running that
playbook at Maccarone — how each piece would go, which §3 principle it's built on, and
what still has to be confirmed. Every figure and name below is a bet the readiness-year
conversations (§5) are meant to test, not a claim we're making.*

## Where it should go

*Runs §2's equity logic — put the park where the underserved kids already are, which is
also where the grants score highest. The open bet: written city site approval, still to win.*

- **Lead candidate: John Maccarone Memorial City Stadium (Morris Ave).** ~19 acres of
  city-owned recreation land, central, with existing parking and ballfields — room to
  add a skate facility without displacing anything, and to phase it. It sits inside a
  New York State–designated Disadvantaged Community, confirmed at 22 Morris Ave on the
  live map. Our access modeling uses this exact location: a 20-minute bike ring from
  here covers roughly 90% of Glen Cove and the DAC core. (Worth a one-minute re-check
  on the live map before any application.)
- **Ruled out: Morgan Memorial Park.** A residents-only, trust-run historic beach — its
  access restriction conflicts with the "free and open to the public" requirement every
  skatepark grant carries. Keep those board relationships for credibility and introductions.
- **Alignment.** The equity case, grant eligibility, our strongest ally (La Fuerza), the
  state's designation, and the access modeling all point to the same central-south part
  of the city. Put the park where the underserved kids already are, and favor a transit-
  and trail-accessible spot.

## What it costs

*Runs the playbook's "phase it" plus the Brentwood cost anchor. The open bet: the real
number — a builder's scoped quote replaces this estimate.*

National industry guidance puts concrete skatepark construction around $50 per square
foot — but that national average understates a Long Island municipal project, where
NY-metro costs, likely prevailing-wage rules, and inflation all push higher. The right
benchmark is our closest real comp: **Brentwood's Roberto Clemente skatepark cost
$1.25 million** for a park in the 8,000–12,000 sq ft range — same island, same
plaza-concrete style, same design firm. So for a neighborhood-scale park of roughly
10,000–12,000 sq ft, **figure on roughly $1.0–1.5 million for the full build**,
with Brentwood's $1.25M in the middle — a band, not a number, until a builder quotes it.

Crucially, that full figure isn't the *first* number we need. Because the park can be
**phased**, a defensible Phase 1 — a smaller but complete, open, usable park — can come
in well under the full build and get kids skating years before the whole vision is
funded (Ketchum and West Bend both did this). Donated city land removes the single
largest line item. A builder's site tour of Maccarone will replace this estimate with a
real, scoped figure — worth doing early, because every funding conversation improves once
we can name a number.

## The funding stack

*Runs the playbook's "layer & sequence the funding" and "private unlocks public (match)."
The open bet: which sources actually land, and in what order — none is committed yet.*

Modeled on Rochester, Jersey City, Brentwood, and West Bend — no single source, layered
*and sequenced over years*. Note: New York's marquee municipal-parks grant (the
Environmental Bond Act MPR) awarded its most recent round in mid-2026, so for now we
*lead* with the legislator-directed DASNY path and a private challenge gift, and get
ready to file when it reopens.

1. **Private lead gift(s)** from Glen Cove's founding funders — the momentum-maker.
   Structure it as a *match* where possible.
2. **The Skatepark Project — Skatepark Grant** (up to $25,000). Nonprofit/municipal
   applicant, concrete, experienced builder, local skaters in design and fundraising,
   no fees, no waivers, underserved area. The credibility-and-modest-cash layer, not
   construction. LOI window January–March (next 2027).
3. **The Skatepark Project — Community Activation Grant** for youth programming (Shane's clinics).
4. **County / state / federal public capital — the big-money layer:**
   - **DASNY — our leading state avenue.** Funded Brentwood; typically directed by a
     state legislator — so our Assembly seat, held by **Charles Lavine (D–Glen Cove)**,
     is the mechanism, and that relationship is the longest-lead move in the plan.
   - **Environmental Bond Act Municipal Parks grant (OPRHP)** — up to **90% of eligible
     costs**, city applies as fee owner, a large share reserved for Disadvantaged
     Communities (our site qualifies). Most recent round awarded mid-2026 — be
     shovel-ready for the next. Its **EPF** sibling (50% match, via the annual CFA) is a
     live complement for the next cycle.
   - **Local Waterfront Revitalization** (if a waterfront-adjacent site); **Land & Water
     Conservation Fund** via the same office.
   - **Nassau County's own** park-capital mechanism (confirm availability); **CDBG**
     through the city's CDA (rolling open round).
5. **A "Friends of" nonprofit** to hold private funds and endow ongoing maintenance. The
   New York Community Trust – Long Island is available as a fundraising home or fiscal
   sponsor alongside La Fuerza.
6. **Private and corporate philanthropy beyond TSP.** The **DICK'S "Game On" program**
   (with LISC), $50k–$100k with a 1:1 match — a fit for a challenge-structured lead gift,
   pending eligibility. The **National Recreation Foundation**. And Long Island's regional
   family foundations — **Rauch, Greentree, Hagedorn, Claire Friedlander** — reachable
   through civic connections.
7. **In-kind and grassroots.** Contractor donations, pro-bono concept work, donor-recognition
   pavers ($500–$1,000 each elsewhere), and skater-run fundraising events, which the
   Skatepark Project specifically wants to see.

**How The Skatepark Project scores applications:** median household income; current access
to skateparks; scope; work already done to fundraise and build awareness; and local data
showing the community is underserved (its definition explicitly includes ethnicity and
language). We now have *two* strong, independent pieces of that local data — the state's
DAC designation and our own access/provisioning analysis. Lead with both.

## The maintenance answer (likely the key to a yes)

*Runs the playbook's "endow the maintenance up front." The open bet: a Friends-of board
that will commit the ~$10k/yr in writing.*

The single biggest reason cities hesitate isn't construction cost — it's the fear of an
ongoing burden. Two facts dissolve it:
- A properly built **concrete** skatepark needs very little upkeep — essentially trash
  collection and occasional cleaning. (This is exactly why grants require concrete.)
- A **Friends of** board can commit to funding even that, for the life of the park. Ann
  Arbor's Friends group took on endowing roughly $10,000 a year as a condition of its
  city deal, so the city carried no ongoing cost. Walking in with an endowed maintenance
  plan — in writing — can turn the city's biggest objection into a reason to say yes.

## If the city asks about supervision

*Runs the playbook's "keep it free" — the rule that outranks all. Not really a bet: free
access is non-negotiable; the only open choice is the supervision model.*

Most grant-funded parks are free and unsupervised (Ketchum; and right here in Nassau
County, Long Beach runs its concrete park free and unfenced). The middle path open to us
is a fence plus **volunteer marshals** from the skate community (Shane's network, the
Friends group) to encourage helmets and etiquette — reassuring the city on liability
without a payroll line. One firm caution: keep it **free — no entry fee, no waiver, no
residency restriction.** Northport's original fee-and-residency setup and Baldwin's
residency requirement are exactly the kind of thing that disqualifies a park from The
Skatepark Project grant.

## Honest watch-outs

*Runs the playbook's "co-investment, not just permission" and "build buy-in early" — the
bets most likely to go wrong, and how we'd hedge each.*

- **Get real city co-investment, not just permission.** In Long Beach, California, the
  city approved a skatepark but contributed no money, leaving the nonprofit to raise
  every dollar. Use private dollars to unlock public dollars (the Ann Arbor match), not
  to let the city off the hook. When the parks grant reopens, the fact that the *city*
  must be the fee-owner applicant gives it a built-in reason to co-invest.
- **Build neighborhood buy-in before you need it.** In NYC, a fully funded $11 million
  skatepark was cut nearly in half after resident pushback. Visible community support
  protects the project's scope.
- **Private board supports; it never gates.** The park must stay free, public, and city-owned.
- **Re-check the designation — and the data — before you cite it.** The site's DAC status
  is confirmed on the live map (July 2026); re-check at submission. Likewise refresh the
  access/provisioning figures against a firmer park census and the live DAC map before
  they go public. (The golf-course count is now established — see below.)
- **The number is an estimate until a builder quotes it.** The ~$1.0–1.5M band is anchored
  on Brentwood's real cost; get the site tour and quote before committing the figure.
- **Pace the volunteers, not just the park.** A multi-year effort's real risk is burnout.
  The antidote is the phased plan — visible wins that keep the coalition energized.

## Suggested sequence

*The order the bets get tested — the hypothesis's experiment plan, not a calendar.*

A realistic arc runs in phases, not on a calendar — the comparable New York projects took
four years (Brentwood) to twelve (Rochester). That length is the shape of the work, and
the reason to begin now.

**Phase 0 — Now (calls, not commitments).** The site's DAC status is confirmed and the
