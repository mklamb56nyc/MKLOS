# A Public Skatepark for Glen Cove
### Strategy brief and case for support

*Re-sequenced July 2026 to lead the way we want to tell it — our kids need this now;
the gap here is acute and unfair; it has been done before and shows us how; we have
the cast to pull it off; and only then, the honest early next steps — with the
working plan (site, cost, funding, maintenance, sequence) kept beneath the narrative
in Part Two. Prepared as a working strategy document and the source for outward-facing
materials. Figures on comparable projects are drawn from public reporting; the DAC
designation, the access/provisioning numbers, the golf-course contrast, the current
park inventory, and the build cost should all be re-verified against the relevant
agencies, a live map check, and a builder before applying or committing.*

====================================================================
PART ONE — THE CASE
====================================================================

## 1. Our kids need this now more than ever

Start with the kid, not the park. This generation is growing up more scheduled,
more screen-bound, and more isolated than any before it — and the years since the
pandemic have only deepened it. What they are short of is unstructured, physical,
face-to-face time in a place that is genuinely their own.

A skatepark is one of the best answers we have. Skateboarding is exercise that
doesn't feel like exercise; it's outdoors; it's creative and self-paced; and it is
radically inclusive — no team to make, no bench, no season, no cut. A kid shows up
at their own level and progresses at their own pace, next to kids who are older,
younger, better, worse, and nothing like them otherwise. It teaches the exact things
we say we want for them: persistence, patience, composure under frustration. You
fall, you get up, you try the thing again. That is the whole sport.

And let's name the old caricature so we can retire it: the skatepark as a den of
smoking, tattoos, and delinquency. That image is decades stale. A well-built modern
skatepark is an all-ages public commons — one of the last "third places" left where
kids gather in person instead of through a screen, build real connection across ages
and backgrounds, and get something increasingly rare: a sense of **ownership** — a
public place that is theirs, that they help design, that they look after because it
belongs to them. That ownership is the point as much as the ramps are.

There's even hard evidence for the skeptic who fears a skatepark invites trouble:
where these parks open, neighborhoods tend to get *safer*, not less safe — in one
Long Beach, California, case, drug-related incidents fell roughly 61% and violent
crime roughly 29% within three years of a park opening. Give kids somewhere to
belong and they use it.

## 2. And here, the need is acute — and unfair

Zoom out and the shortage is measurable, not rhetorical. New York State is
under-parked for skateboarding; Nassau County acutely so; and our own Glen Cove /
Oyster Bay stretch of the North Shore most of all.

**Provisioning — starved by the field's own standard.** The Public Skatepark
Development Guide (the industry reference planners use) sets a benchmark of one
neighborhood skatepark per 25,000 residents. By that standard Nassau County should
have about 56 and has **4**; Long Island should have about 117 and has **14**; our
immediate North Shore area should have about 3 and has **none**. And this isn't just
falling short of an ideal nobody meets: the *national average* is roughly one
skatepark per 100,000 people, and Nassau sits at about a quarter of that — fewer
skateparks per person than the typical American community.

**Access — the people who'd use it can't reach one.** Because the actual users are
kids who mostly can't drive, the honest test is whether a young person can get there
on their own. Today, **100% of Glen Cove's residents — and 100% of the state-designated
Disadvantaged Community area — are outside a 20-minute trip to any skatepark, by bike
or by car**, and that stays true even if Bethpage's shuttered park reopens. Zoom out
and 77% of Nassau County and 97% of the Town of Oyster Bay can't *bike* to one. A
single park at the Maccarone site closes roughly **90% of that access gap** for Glen
Cove and the DAC core, and brings tens of thousands more across the Town of Oyster
Bay into range. Because Bethpage's reach never touches the North Shore, our park's
value to our own community is identical whether or not Bethpage ever comes back.

**Now make it personal, because the numbers understate the injustice.** The kids who
need this most live in our downtown/south core — a New York State–designated
Disadvantaged Community, confirmed at the lead site. And here is the contrast that
should stop a reader cold: within a short drive of those neighborhoods sit roughly
half a dozen private eighteen-hole golf courses — hundreds of acres of manicured,
gated, private recreation — while the nearest *public* skatepark those kids can use
is a twenty-five-minute drive away. A golf course is recreation for someone with a
membership. A skatepark is recreation for a kid with a board and a bike. And the
drive is the trap: "twenty-five minutes away" really means "reachable only with a
parent who has a car and the free time to make the trip" — exactly what many of
these families don't have. The kids with the least are asked to travel the farthest
for the simplest thing.

*Translation for a grant reviewer or a council member: this is precisely the "local
data showing the community is underserved" that funders score on, and it pairs with
the state's DAC designation to make the equity case twice over.*

## 3. This has been done — and it shows us how

None of this is uncharted. Every move in this brief is modeled on a real project that
succeeded, and together they're a how-to.

**The flagship national examples — the inspiration and the playbook.**
- **Berry Lane Park, Jersey City NJ** — a 17-acre toxic brownfield turned into a
  lower-income neighborhood's premier park (opened 2020); a Skatepark Project grant
  plus a $500,000 county Open Space grant, designed by SITE Design Group with local
  skaters throughout. Almost our exact story, told as a win.
- **Long Beach, CA (the ASK model)** — youth at the center of the design, and the
  measured drop in neighborhood crime that answers a city's safety worries head-on.
- **Ketchum, ID (Guy Coles)** — a small, affluent town's *free* concrete park grown
  in phases over decades on modest city money, a skatepark trust fund, and local
  philanthropy: our template for building a strong Phase 1, opening it, and letting a
  used park raise money for the next.

**Closer to home — the New York State examples that prove it fits here.**
- **Rochester, NY (ROC City)** — the funding-stack playbook: state revitalization
  money, a separate $3M state grant, utility and Skatepark Project grants, a family
  foundation that gave unrestricted money *and* matched every dollar, and a "Friends
  of" nonprofit. No single source carried it — and it was a twelve-year haul, which
  is the honest shape of the work.
- **Brentwood, NY (Roberto Clemente Park)** — the closest match of all: one of Long
  Island's working-class, majority-Latino communities opened a free concrete skatepark
  on a former toxic-dumping site (conceived 2019, opened 2023). Youth-led — a
  high-school senior's petition moved a state assemblyman to secure legislator-directed
  funding plus a $450,000 DASNY grant — designed by the firm we'd call first (SITE
  Design Group) and built for $1.25M, our best real-world cost anchor.
- **Long Beach, NY** — the *other* independent city in Nassau County, same governance
  as Glen Cove, running a free, unfenced concrete park designed through public
  workshops. Proof a city of our exact type gets this done, our way.
- **Manorhaven / Port Washington, NY** — a North Shore Nassau municipality that built
  a free ~10,000 sq ft concrete park with local-skater input (designed by Spohn Ranch),
  and reopened it after the pandemic when a teenager petitioned. Our nearest western
  neighbor and a same-county governance-and-design comp.

**More proof, in brief:**
- **West Bend, WI (2026):** a "Friends of" nonprofit and the city secured a $500,000
  *match* commitment conditional on raising an equal amount, plus a donor-funded design.
- **Ann Arbor, MI:** the county pledged up to $400,000 in matching funds conditional
  on private fundraising — private money triggering public money.
- **Riverside Park, NYC:** at-risk teens co-designed and helped build a park created
  for adolescents "designed out" of public space.
- **Hoboken, NJ (2025):** a 250-person community survey drove the design and drew a
  $250,000 county open-space grant.
- **Northport, NY (Veterans Skatepark):** the Long Island example of the
  supervised-access model (fenced, staffed for helmets) — and our eastern "nearest park."

**The ceiling and the caution — Charleston, SC (SK8 Charleston).** A $4.8M,
32,500 sq ft county destination park that charges admission and requires a waiver
behind a fenced gate — and is therefore *not* grant-eligible under free-access rules.
The aspiration, and the reminder to keep ours free.

## 4. We have the cast to pull it off

We are not starting from nothing. There is a remarkable set of people, institutions,
and programs already within reach — the ingredients of a coalition.

- **The people.** A family ready to quarterback it; Glen Cove benefactor friends who
  could be the founding funders and a "Friends of" board (and the door-openers to
  Long Island's regional foundations); **Shane McGrane**, a credible local skater and
  the son's coach, to lead skater-driven design (a grant requirement) and run youth
  clinics that open a second funding stream; and **La Fuerza Unida**, an established
  Latino-serving nonprofit downtown — in the same designated community as the site —
  as a community anchor and possible nonprofit vehicle. On top of a ready-made base:
  1,200+ signatures from a dormant 2020 petition and the organized Bethpage Skatepark
  Coalition next door.
- **The institutions.** Glen Cove is an independent city that controls its own
  parkland and budget, with a Recreation Commission as the natural first door and a
  Community Development Agency that has an in-house senior grant writer. At the state
  and county level, Assemblymember **Charles Lavine** is our primary path to directed
  state capital (DASNY), with County Legislator **Delia DeRiggi-Whitton** a natural ally.
- **The programs and funders.** The Skatepark Project (construction and youth-program
  grants); DICK'S / LISC "Game On" ($50k–$100k against a 1:1 match); the New York
  Community Trust – Long Island as a fundraising home or fiscal sponsor; and Long
  Island's regional family foundations — Rauch, Greentree, Hagedorn, Claire Friedlander
  — reachable through civic connections. Plus experienced concrete builders (SITE
  Design Group, Spohn Ranch) with Long Island track records to turn a vision into a park.

*Two honest notes we keep in front of us. First: almost none of these people have been
asked yet — this is the cast that **could** help, not a list of supporters, and nobody
gets represented as on board until they've actually said yes. Second, on optics: since
our family lives in Locust Valley rather than Glen Cove, Glen Cove residents should be
the public face, with the family organizing behind them. (Locust Valley has a genuine
tie regardless — Morgan Park's charter serves both, and the 2020 petition named Locust
Valley as part of the same community.)*

## 5. This is real, early — the crucial next steps

Told plainly: this is a multi-year, phased effort — the comparable New York projects
took four years (Brentwood) to twelve (Rochester). We're at the very start, and 2026
is a readiness year: line everything up so the city can file the moment the next state
grant window opens (~2027). The goal now isn't to build a park; it's the handful of
discovery steps that unlock everything after them.

1. **Get a real, scoped build-cost number** — a builder's site tour of the Maccarone
   site (SITE Design Group / Spohn Ranch first). The single biggest gap; every funding
   conversation improves the moment we can name a figure.
2. **Win written city site approval** — via the Recreation Commission and mayor's
   office. The critical-path gate; nothing downstream moves until it's in hand.
3. **Begin cultivating Assemblymember Lavine** — the DASNY path is our leading state
   avenue and the longest-lead relationship, so it has to start earliest.
4. **Have the first warm conversations** — the 2020 petition's organizer, La Fuerza
   Unida, Shane, and two or three benefactor friends — to turn a case into a coalition.
5. **Firm up the underserved exhibit** — a firmer LI / Nassau skatepark census and a
   re-verification of the DAC tracts on the live state map, so the numbers are
   submission-ready.

The people who start this now become its founders.

====================================================================
PART TWO — THE WORKING PLAN
====================================================================

## Where it should go

- **Lead candidate: John Maccarone Memorial City Stadium (Morris Ave).** ~19 acres of
  city-owned recreation land, central, with existing parking and ballfields — room to
  add a skate facility without displacing anything, and to phase it. It sits inside a
  New York State–designated Disadvantaged Community, confirmed at 22 Morris Ave on the
  live map. Our access modeling uses this exact location: a 20-minute bike ring from
  here covers roughly 90% of Glen Cove and the DAC core. (Worth a one-minute re-check
  on the live map before any application.)
- **Ruled out: Morgan Memorial Park.** A residents-only, trust-run historic beach — its
  access restriction conflicts with the "free and open to the public" requirement every
  skatepark grant carries. Keep those board relationships for credibility and introductions.
- **Alignment.** The equity case, grant eligibility, our strongest ally (La Fuerza), the
  state's designation, and the access modeling all point to the same central-south part
  of the city. Put the park where the underserved kids already are, and favor a transit-
  and trail-accessible spot.

## What it costs

National industry guidance puts concrete skatepark construction around $50 per square
foot — but that national average understates a Long Island municipal project, where
NY-metro costs, likely prevailing-wage rules, and inflation all push higher. The right
benchmark is our closest real comp: **Brentwood's Roberto Clemente skatepark cost
$1.25 million** for a park in the 8,000–12,000 sq ft range — same island, same
plaza-concrete style, same design firm. So for a neighborhood-scale park of roughly
10,000–12,000 sq ft, **plan on approximately $1.0–1.5 million for the full build**,
with Brentwood's $1.25M in the middle.

Crucially, that full figure isn't the *first* number we need. Because the park can be
**phased**, a defensible Phase 1 — a smaller but complete, open, usable park — can come
in well under the full build and get kids skating years before the whole vision is
funded (Ketchum and West Bend both did this). Donated city land removes the single
largest line item. A builder's site tour of Maccarone will replace this estimate with a
real, scoped figure — worth doing early, because every funding conversation improves once
we can name a number.

## The funding stack

Modeled on Rochester, Jersey City, Brentwood, and West Bend — no single source, layered
*and sequenced over years*. Note: New York's marquee municipal-parks grant (the
Environmental Bond Act MPR) awarded its most recent round in mid-2026, so for now we
*lead* with the legislator-directed DASNY path and a private challenge gift, and get
ready to file when it reopens.

1. **Private lead gift(s)** from Glen Cove's founding funders — the momentum-maker.
   Structure it as a *match* where possible.
2. **The Skatepark Project — Skatepark Grant** (up to $25,000). Nonprofit/municipal
   applicant, concrete, experienced builder, local skaters in design and fundraising,
   no fees, no waivers, underserved area. The credibility-and-modest-cash layer, not
   construction. LOI window January–March (next 2027).
3. **The Skatepark Project — Community Activation Grant** for youth programming (Shane's clinics).
4. **County / state / federal public capital — the big-money layer:**
   - **DASNY — our leading state avenue.** Funded Brentwood; typically directed by a
     state legislator — so our Assembly seat, held by **Charles Lavine (D–Glen Cove)**,
     is the mechanism, and that relationship is the longest-lead move in the plan.
   - **Environmental Bond Act Municipal Parks grant (OPRHP)** — up to **90% of eligible
     costs**, city applies as fee owner, a large share reserved for Disadvantaged
     Communities (our site qualifies). Most recent round awarded mid-2026 — be
     shovel-ready for the next. Its **EPF** sibling (50% match, via the annual CFA) is a
     live complement for the next cycle.
   - **Local Waterfront Revitalization** (if a waterfront-adjacent site); **Land & Water
     Conservation Fund** via the same office.
   - **Nassau County's own** park-capital mechanism (confirm availability); **CDBG**
     through the city's CDA (rolling open round).
5. **A "Friends of" nonprofit** to hold private funds and endow ongoing maintenance. The
   New York Community Trust – Long Island is available as a fundraising home or fiscal
   sponsor alongside La Fuerza.
6. **Private and corporate philanthropy beyond TSP.** The **DICK'S "Game On" program**
   (with LISC), $50k–$100k with a 1:1 match — a fit for a challenge-structured lead gift,
   pending eligibility. The **National Recreation Foundation**. And Long Island's regional
   family foundations — **Rauch, Greentree, Hagedorn, Claire Friedlander** — reachable
   through civic connections.
7. **In-kind and grassroots.** Contractor donations, pro-bono concept work, donor-recognition
   pavers ($500–$1,000 each elsewhere), and skater-run fundraising events, which the
   Skatepark Project specifically wants to see.

**How The Skatepark Project scores applications:** median household income; current access
to skateparks; scope; work already done to fundraise and build awareness; and local data
showing the community is underserved (its definition explicitly includes ethnicity and
language). We now have *two* strong, independent pieces of that local data — the state's
DAC designation and our own access/provisioning analysis. Lead with both.

## The maintenance answer (this is what wins the city over)

The single biggest reason cities hesitate isn't construction cost — it's the fear of an
ongoing burden. Two facts dissolve it:
- A properly built **concrete** skatepark needs very little upkeep — essentially trash
  collection and occasional cleaning. (This is exactly why grants require concrete.)
- A **Friends of** board can commit to funding even that, for the life of the park. Ann
  Arbor's Friends group took on endowing roughly $10,000 a year as a condition of its
  city deal, so the city carried no ongoing cost. Walking in with an endowed maintenance
  plan — in writing — turns the city's biggest objection into a reason to say yes.

## If the city asks about supervision

Most grant-funded parks are free and unsupervised (Ketchum; and right here in Nassau
County, Long Beach runs its concrete park free and unfenced). The middle path open to us
is a fence plus **volunteer marshals** from the skate community (Shane's network, the
Friends group) to encourage helmets and etiquette — reassuring the city on liability
without a payroll line. One firm caution: keep it **free — no entry fee, no waiver, no
residency restriction.** Northport's original fee-and-residency setup and Baldwin's
residency requirement are exactly the kind of thing that disqualifies a park from The
Skatepark Project grant.

## Honest watch-outs

- **Get real city co-investment, not just permission.** In Long Beach, California, the
  city approved a skatepark but contributed no money, leaving the nonprofit to raise
  every dollar. Use private dollars to unlock public dollars (the Ann Arbor match), not
  to let the city off the hook. When the parks grant reopens, the fact that the *city*
  must be the fee-owner applicant gives it a built-in reason to co-invest.
- **Build neighborhood buy-in before you need it.** In NYC, a fully funded $11 million
  skatepark was cut nearly in half after resident pushback. Visible community support
  protects the project's scope.
- **Private board supports; it never gates.** The park must stay free, public, and city-owned.
- **Re-check the designation — and the data — before you cite it.** The site's DAC status
  is confirmed on the live map (July 2026); re-check at submission. Likewise refresh the
  access/provisioning figures — and the golf-course count — against a firmer park census
  and the live DAC map before they go public.
- **The number is an estimate until a builder quotes it.** The ~$1.0–1.5M band is anchored
  on Brentwood's real cost; get the site tour and quote before committing the figure.
- **Pace the volunteers, not just the park.** A multi-year effort's real risk is burnout.
  The antidote is the phased plan — visible wins that keep the coalition energized.

## Suggested sequence

A realistic arc runs in phases, not on a calendar — the comparable New York projects took
four years (Brentwood) to twelve (Rochester). That length is the shape of the work, and
the reason to begin now.

**Phase 0 — Now (calls, not commitments).** The site's DAC status is confirmed and the
underserved case is quantified. First conversations with the 2020 petition's organizer, La
Fuerza, Shane, and two or three benefactor friends. Learn the Bethpage rebuild timeline.
Begin cultivating Assemblymember Lavine — our primary path to state capital and the
longest-lead relationship.

**Phase 1 — Form up (the readiness stretch).** Stand up the coalition and a "Friends of"
vehicle. Secure a lead gift, ideally a match. Settle on the site, and get two or three
concrete builders to tour it for a real cost number. Firm up the supply census and
re-verify the DAC tracts for the underserved exhibit. Begin approaching the regional
Long Island foundations.

**Phase 2 — Approach the city (the gate).** Bring the Recreation Commission and mayor's
office a specific site, a real coalition, partial funding, a real cost figure, an endowed
maintenance plan, and the access/provisioning analysis. Secure written site approval and
press for co-investment. Nothing downstream moves until this is in hand.

**Phase 3 — Fund and design (the funding years).** Apply to The Skatepark Project plus
New York State/county capital — leading with DASNY through Lavine, filing for the Bond Act
Municipal Parks grant when it reopens (and EPF in the next cycle), with the DICK'S/LISC
grant paired with the match. Hire an experienced concrete design-build firm and run
skater-led design workshops.

**Phase 4 — Build, program, open (possibly in phases).** Construct — as one build or a
Phase 1 that grows — launch Shane's youth clinics, and hold a grand opening that credits
every partner.
