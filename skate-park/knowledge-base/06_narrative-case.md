# A Skatepark for Glen Cove — the case, our way

*A narrative framing of the campaign, ordered the way we want to tell it: why our
kids need this now, why the need here is both acute and unfair, that it has been
done before (and shows us how), who could help us pull it off, and only then the
honest, early next steps. Companion to the strategy brief (`04`) and the intended
spine for outward-facing materials. Figures trace to the research dossier (`01`)
and the analysis bundle; re-verify the flagged items before anything goes public.*

---

## 1. Our kids need this now more than ever

Start with the kid, not the park. This generation is growing up more scheduled,
more screen-bound, and more isolated than any before it — and the years since the
pandemic have only deepened it. What they are short of is unstructured, physical,
face-to-face time in a place that is genuinely their own.

A skatepark is one of the best answers we have to that. Skateboarding is exercise
that doesn't feel like exercise; it's outdoors; it's creative and self-paced; and
it is radically inclusive — there's no team to make, no bench, no season, no cut.
A kid shows up at their own level and progresses at their own pace, next to kids
who are older, younger, better, worse, and nothing like them otherwise. It teaches
the exact things we say we want for them: persistence, patience, composure under
frustration. You fall, you get up, you try the thing again. That is the whole sport.

And let's name the old caricature so we can retire it: the skatepark as a den of
smoking, tattoos, and delinquency. That image is decades stale. A well-built modern
skatepark is a supervised-by-community, all-ages public space — one of the last
"third places" left where kids gather in person instead of through a screen. Where
they build real, face-to-face connection across ages and backgrounds. And where
they get something increasingly rare: a sense of **ownership** — a public place
that is theirs, that they help design, that they look after because it belongs to
them. That ownership is the point as much as the ramps are.

There's even hard evidence for the skeptics who worry a skatepark invites trouble:
where these parks have opened, neighborhoods have gotten *safer*, not less safe —
in one Long Beach, California, case, drug-related incidents fell roughly 61% and
violent crime roughly 29% within three years of a park opening. Give kids somewhere
to belong and they use it.

## 2. And here, the need is acute — and unfair

Zoom out and the shortage is measurable, not rhetorical. New York State is
under-parked for skateboarding; Nassau County acutely so; and our own
Glen Cove / Oyster Bay stretch of the North Shore most of all. By the field's own
planning standard (one neighborhood skatepark per 25,000 residents), Nassau County
should have about 56 and has **4**; Long Island should have about 117 and has
**14**; our immediate area should have about 3 and has **none**. Put against the
*national* average of roughly one skatepark per 100,000 people, Nassau sits at
about a quarter of the norm — not short of an ideal nobody meets, but below what
the typical American community already has.

Now make it personal, because the raw shortage understates the injustice. The kids
who need this most are the ones in our downtown/south core — a New York
State–designated Disadvantaged Community, confirmed at the lead site. And here is
the contrast that should stop a reader cold: within a short drive of those
neighborhoods sit roughly half a dozen private eighteen-hole golf courses —
hundreds of acres of manicured, gated, private recreation — while the nearest
*public* skatepark those kids can use is a twenty-five-minute drive away. A golf
course is recreation for someone with a membership. A skatepark is recreation for
a kid with a board and a bike.

And the drive is the trap. The actual users are children who mostly can't drive, so
"twenty-five minutes away" really means "reachable only with a parent who has a car
and the free time to make the trip" — exactly what many of these families don't
have. Measured honestly by whether a young person can get there on their own,
**100% of Glen Cove and 100% of the designated Disadvantaged Community area are
outside a 20-minute trip — by bike or by car — to any skatepark at all**, and that
stays true even if Bethpage's shuttered park someday reopens. The kids with the
least are asked to travel the farthest for the simplest thing.

## 3. The good news: this has been done — and it shows us how

None of this is uncharted. Every move we'd make is modeled on a real project that
succeeded, and together they're a how-to.

**The flagship national examples — the inspiration and the playbook.**
- **Berry Lane Park, Jersey City NJ** — a 17-acre toxic brownfield turned into a
  lower-income neighborhood's premier park. Almost our exact story, told as a win.
- **Long Beach, CA (the ASK model)** — youth at the center of the design, and the
  measured drop in neighborhood crime that answers a city's safety worries head-on.
- **Ketchum, ID** — a small town's *free* concrete park grown in phases over years:
  our template for building a strong Phase 1, opening it, and letting a used park
  raise money for the next.

**Closer to home — the New York State examples that prove it fits here.**
- **Rochester, NY (ROC City)** — the funding-stack playbook: state money, a separate
  large state grant, foundation match, a "Friends of" nonprofit. No single source
  carried it — and it was a twelve-year haul, which is the honest shape of the work.
- **Brentwood, NY (Roberto Clemente Park)** — the closest match of all: one of
  Long Island's working-class, majority-Latino communities opened a free concrete
  skatepark on a former toxic-dumping site, youth-led, funded through a state
  legislator's DASNY grant, built by the firm we'd call first — for $1.25M, our best
  real-world cost anchor.
- **Long Beach, NY** — the *other* independent city in Nassau County, same
  governance as Glen Cove, running a free, unfenced concrete park. Proof a city of
  our exact type gets this done.
- **Manorhaven / Port Washington, NY** — a North Shore Nassau municipality that
  built a free ~10,000 sq ft concrete park with local-skater input, and reopened it
  when a teenager petitioned. Our nearest western neighbor and a same-county comp.

## 4. And we have the cast to pull it off

We are not starting from nothing. There is a remarkable set of people, institutions,
and programs already in reach — the ingredients of a coalition.

- **The people.** A family ready to quarterback it; Glen Cove benefactor friends who
  could be the founding funders and a "Friends of" board; Shane McGrane, a credible
  local skater and coach to lead skater-driven design and run youth clinics; and
  **La Fuerza Unida**, an established Latino-serving nonprofit downtown — in the same
  designated community as the site — as a community anchor and possible nonprofit
  vehicle. On top of a ready-made base: 1,200+ signatures from a dormant 2020
  petition and the organized Bethpage Skatepark Coalition next door.
- **The institutions.** Glen Cove is an independent city that controls its own
  parkland and budget, with a Recreation Commission as the natural first door and a
  Community Development Agency that has an in-house senior grant writer. At the state
  and county level, Assemblymember **Charles Lavine** is our primary path to
  directed state capital (DASNY), with County Legislator **Delia DeRiggi-Whitton** a
  natural ally.
- **The programs and funders.** The Skatepark Project (construction and youth-program
  grants); DICK'S / LISC "Game On" ($50k–$100k against a match); the New York
  Community Trust – Long Island as a fundraising home; and Long Island's regional
  family foundations — Rauch, Greentree, Hagedorn, Claire Friedlander — reachable
  through civic connections. Plus experienced concrete builders (SITE Design Group,
  Spohn Ranch) with Long Island track records who can turn a vision into a real park.

*An honest note we keep in front of us: almost none of these people have been asked
yet. This is the cast that **could** help — not a list of supporters. Nobody gets
represented as on board until they've actually said yes.*

## 5. This is real, early, and here's what we do first

Told plainly: this is a multi-year, phased effort — the comparable New York projects
took four years (Brentwood) to twelve (Rochester). We're at the very start, and 2026
is a readiness year: line everything up so the city can file the moment the next
state grant window opens. The goal now isn't to build a park; it's to do the handful
of discovery steps that unlock everything after them.

The most crucial next steps:
1. **Get a real, scoped build-cost number** — a builder's site tour of the Maccarone
   site (SITE Design Group / Spohn Ranch first). This is the single biggest gap;
   every funding conversation improves the moment we can name a figure.
2. **Win written city site approval** — via the Recreation Commission and mayor's
   office. It's the critical-path gate; nothing downstream moves until it's in hand.
3. **Begin cultivating Assemblymember Lavine** — the DASNY path is our leading state
   avenue and the longest-lead relationship, so it has to start earliest.
4. **Have the first warm conversations** — the 2020 petition's organizer, La Fuerza
   Unida, Shane, and two or three benefactor friends — to turn a case into a coalition.
5. **Firm up the underserved exhibit** — a firmer Long Island / Nassau skatepark
   census and a re-verification of the Disadvantaged Community tracts on the live
   state map, so the numbers are submission-ready.

The people who start this now become its founders.
