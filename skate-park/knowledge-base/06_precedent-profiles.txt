====================================================================
CROSS-CUTTING LESSONS (read this first)
====================================================================
- EVERY project had (a) one relentless inside-or-outside champion who stayed for years,
  and (b) a youth/community face. None was carried by a single funding source.
- "Approval without appropriation" is the recurring trap (Long Beach CA Silverado sat
  fully-approved and $0-funded ~18 months; Jersey City's skatepark waited 4+ years behind
  other phases). Get a city dollar commitment, not a blessing.
- County open-space trust money is the repeat load-bearing line item in NY-metro stacks
  (Berry Lane $500K; Hoboken $500K) — Nassau's equivalent is a primary target to confirm.
- Small national grants (TSP $15–25K) never build the park; they anchor credibility that
  attracts the big public money. Sequence them first anyway.
- Youth petitions repeatedly moved governments (Brentwood; Manorhaven's reopening at 742
  signatures; Riverside's volunteer-era revival) — our dormant 1,200+ is a real asset.
- Concrete-vs-wood is a maintenance decision with decades of consequences: Riverside's
  wood rotted into near-removal; Manorhaven's concrete needed no capital attention 2011–25.
- Unsupervised facilities are the first thing a risk-averse government closes and the last
  it reopens (Manorhaven 2020–21) — the volunteer-marshal middle path pre-answers this.

1. MANORHAVEN / PORT WASHINGTON, NY — "North Hempstead's Skate Spot" (nearest west)
====================================================================
WHO DECIDED: ⚠ CORRECTION — the TOWN OF NORTH HEMPSTEAD, not the Village of Manorhaven.
The park sits inside the Village geographically but is town-owned/operated; every decision
(siting, funding, design, 2020 closure, 2021 reopening) was made by Town officials.
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manorhaven_Beach_Park
  https://www.northhempsteadny.gov/departments/parks_recreation/parks_directory/manorhaven_beach_park.php
TIMELINE (~4 yrs advocacy→open): 2007 SNAPP ("Skaters Need a Park in Port") founded by
Howard & Karen Cohen (Robert Laker later president) → Apr 28 2010 Town siting meeting
(Supervisor Jon Kaiman, Councilman Fred Pollack) → Dec 2010 Spohn Ranch designer Jason
Baldessari works directly with teen skaters → built 2011, grand opening Oct 23, 2011.
  https://patch.com/new-york/portwashington/port-skateboarders-gear-up-for-skate-park-meeting
  https://patch.com/new-york/portwashington/new-skate-spot-in-port-washington-video
SIZE/SURFACE/BUILDER: ~10,000 sq ft concrete (street + transition, raised central plaza,
bowl feature, lights). Design-build: Spohn Ranch (Jason Baldessari), with teen input.
SNAPP had wanted 15,000 sq ft; the Town built smaller — hence "Skate Spot" branding.
COST & FUNDING: contract just under $349,000 (2011), funded by a Town bond issue for park
improvements — single-source, no county/state/village money in any coverage. (Whether a
line inside the concurrent $12M Manorhaven Beach Park renovation bond: UNVERIFIED.)
KEY PEOPLE: the Cohens/SNAPP (parent-led engine); Councilman Fred Pollack (political
champion, brokered teen-designer collaboration); Supervisor Jon Kaiman (convened siting);
teen campaigners (press dubbed them "Roll Models"). 2021 reopening: Jett Gillis, 13
(petition, 742 signatures final), Councilwoman Mariann Dalimonte (struck the deal).
GOVERNANCE PATH: Town Board/Supervisor throughout — public siting meeting → design meeting
→ ~$349K bond-funded contract → town-run operation. Closure/reopening were administrative
decisions, not board votes. (Specific 2010-11 contract resolution: UNVERIFIED — minutes
not online.)
ACCESS MODEL TODAY: free to skate, unsupervised; park hours 7am–10pm in season.
⚠ FLAG FOR OUR CENSUS: the Town enforces a general residency-ID policy for park admission
(in force since 1946 per Wikipedia/town page), and parking is fee-based. If the Skate Spot
actually sits behind a residency gate, it would fail our own free-access rule (as we
applied to Huntington) and Nassau's count drops 3→2 — which would make our shortfall case
STRONGER but change the census. VERIFY on the ground / by phone before publishing counts.
  https://www.northhempsteadny.gov/departments/parks_recreation/parks_directory/manorhaven_beach_park.php
STATUS: open, active town amenity; no renovation/controversy coverage 2024–25.
NEARLY DIED / LESSONS: Nov 23 2020 the Town closed it "indefinitely" (COVID congregating);
Supervisor refused to reopen; a 13-year-old's petition + one councilmember produced the
"skate-park ambassadors" deal and a spring-2021 reopening. Lessons: (1) unsupervised parks
are the first closed, last reopened — the Town literally paid guards to turn skaters away;
(2) both the creation and the rescue were kid/parent pressure aimed at the CORRECT
jurisdiction plus one willing councilmember; (3) concrete's near-zero maintenance is why
it needed no capital attention for 14 years.

2. LONG BEACH, CA — THE ASK FOUNDATION MODEL (safety evidence + the $0-city watch-out)
====================================================================
WHO DECIDED: City of Long Beach (council + Parks, Recreation & Marine Dept); ASK
(Action Sports Kids Foundation, 501(c)(3), EIN 45-2300860, founded 2011 by former
Councilman Mike Donelon) is the private advocacy/fundraising engine, not the landowner.
  https://www.asklongbeach.org/about  https://projects.propublica.org/nonprofits/organizations/452300860
TIMELINE: 1996 Donelon (then on council) identifies need → 1998 first feasibility study →
2000 El Dorado (city's first) → 2002/03 14th St plaza opens (⚠ sources conflict 2002 vs
2003) → Apr 9 2005 Michael Keith Green, 20, sponsored young pro, killed in gang crossfire
(not gang-affiliated) → Jan 2010 park expanded + unanimously renamed Michael K. Green
Skate Park (Donelon petition, 1,000+ signatures) → 2011 ASK founded → Silverado remodel:
vision ~2010, launched 2021, funded late 2023, reopened Feb 24 2024.
  https://sigtrib.com/skate-park-renamed-after-young-man-killed-in-gang-crossfire/
  https://lbpost.com/news/photos-silverado-skatepark-reopens-after-153k-upgrade/
⚠ CORRECTION: "Michael K. Green skatepark opened 2003" conflates two facts — the plaza
opened 2002/03 unnamed; the Green name + expanded facility date to Jan 2010.
THE CRIME STATISTIC (brief §1d) — CITE WITH CARE:
- Canonical (ASK's wording): within three years of the 2003 opening, drug-related
  incidents −60.9%, violent crime −29.3%, overall incidents −22.8% — IN THE AREA AROUND
  THE PARK (roughly the 90813 zip), not citywide. https://www.asklongbeach.org/about
- ⚠ No published LBPD report/study could be located; every citation chain terminates at
  ASK/Donelon. Precise decimals suggest a real LBPD data pull, but treat as
  ASK-REPORTED, single-park, correlational. Defensible phrasing: "ASK Foundation reports
  that in the three years after the park opened, drug-related incidents in the
  surrounding area fell ~61%, violent crime ~29%, overall ~23%."
- Safer companion stat: TSP's law-enforcement survey — nearly half of officers surveyed
  reported reduced youth crime after a skatepark opened.
  https://publicskateparkguide.org/advocacy/thf-police-study/
SILVERADO — THE WATCH-OUT (verified): Parks Dept approved the vision plan but contributed
$0; ASK had to raise everything. The project sat ~18 months approved-but-unfunded until
Nouns DAO (on-chain Prop 303, co-branded with TSP) gave $133,250 (~86% of the total);
final stack ≈$153–155K: Nouns $133,250 + TSP $15,000 + Port of Long Beach $5,000 +
fireworks-show proceeds $5,000. Youth design: 150+ Long Beach youth shaped Silverado
specifically (don't generalize the "150 kids" claim to all parks).
  https://lbpost.com/news/tony-hawk-silverado-skatepark-project/
  https://lbpost.com/news/westside-skate-park-silverado-remodel-reopen/
KEY PEOPLE: Mike Donelon (25+ years on one issue: council feasibility study → nine-ish
parks → ASK; the entire model is his persistence); Tony Hawk/TSP; Geoff Rowley (designed
Bixby, Vans-funded). ASK 2022 revenue just $141K — the leverage is the network, not the
balance sheet. Named 2026 CA Nonprofit of the Year (nom. Asm. Josh Lowenthal).
STATUS: ~9–10 city skateparks (counts vary by source); 2024–25 citywide feasibility study
(631 surveyed, 90% pro); proposed ~$5M Alamitos Beach destination park called "a long
shot." ACCESS: city-run; free/unsupervised is the likely default but UNVERIFIED (city
page 503'd during research).
LESSONS: (1) the $0-city posture is the model's chief fragility — approval without
appropriation strands projects; (2) an unconventional funder (a crypto DAO) rescued
Silverado — no plan could have counted on that; (3) one champion who never leaves the
issue compounds for decades; (4) facts drift in retellings — cite the primary-est source.

3. BERRY LANE PARK SKATEPARK — JERSEY CITY, NJ (the brownfield-equity flagship)
====================================================================
WHO DECIDED: City of Jersey City, delivered through the Jersey City Redevelopment Agency
(JCRA) as the final phase of the 17.5-acre Berry Lane Park redevelopment (Bergen-
Lafayette / Communipaw, the city's underserved south side).
  https://www.jerseycitynj.gov/news/pressreleases2020/jcopensskateparkwithtonyhawkfoundationgrant
  https://thejcra.org/berry-lane-park-environmental-investigation-and-remediation-summary/
TIMELINE (~5.5 yrs grant→open): Tony Hawk Foundation top-tier grant Mar 2015 →
construction announced Aug 2019 → groundbreaking Nov 2019 (with 2,113 sq ft LEED-Silver
pavilion) → opened early Aug 2020 (COVID slipped spring target). Parent park: remediation
began Aug 2012, park opened Jun 2016; skatepark was phase six of six.
  https://juicemagazine.com/home/jersey-city-receives-tony-hawk-foundation-top-skatepark-grant/
SIZE/SURFACE/BUILDER: ~12,000 sq ft poured-in-place concrete — first in Hudson County,
largest in JC (layback bank, quarter pipes, A-frames, ~6-ft bowl). Designer: SITE Design
Group (secondary-sourced attribution). Builder: Tsivikos Enterprises (pavilion: ML Inc.).
COST & FUNDING: ⚠ CORRECTION/nuance — skatepark+pavilion job ≈ $2.8M combined (NJBIZ);
a conflicting $250K figure attaches to the Tsivikos skatepark contract (seems low for
12,000 sq ft; split UNVERIFIED — flag both, don't cite a single number).
Stack: TSP/THF $25,000 (top-tier, Winter 2015 — ⚠ our brief had no amount) + Hudson
County Open Space Trust Fund $500,000 + city capital via JCRA (amount UNVERIFIED).
Parent park ~$38–40M total: PPG chromium-settlement money, EPA brownfield, HUD CDBG,
NJ EDA $5M, NJDEP, county — city paid only ~15%.
  https://njbiz.com/jersey-city-breaks-ground-berry-lane-skate-park/
  https://www.cdfa.net/cdfa/cdfaweb.nsf/ord/911C5C4221C4833F88257DBD007102AA/$file/Berry-Lane-Park.pdf
BROWNFIELD STORY: 11 former industrial properties; three NJDEP chromium sites within the
footprint (PPG's Garfield Ave plant); JCRA remediation 2012–13; PPG separately removed
~700,000 tons of chromium waste from area sites.
KEY PEOPLE: Mayor Steven Fulop (public champion, framed as equity investment); Diana
Jeffrey (JCRA ED, delivery); Ward F Councilman Jermaine Robinson; THF's Miki Vuckovich +
Tony Hawk. Named grassroots advocates: UNVERIFIED.
ACCESS: free, open 8am–9pm, lit; run by JC Recreation. STATUS: open; NJ Grind (2025)
calls it arguably the best in NJ.
NEARLY DIED / LESSONS: (1) the enemy was the WAIT, not opposition — the 2015 seed grant
anchored a claim while big public money assembled behind five other phases; (2) the
county open-space trust was the load-bearing skatepark line; (3) attaching the skatepark
to a flagship equity project gave electeds a reason to own it publicly.

4. MARITIME PARK SKATEPARK — HOBOKEN, NJ (the survey model; NOT yet open)
====================================================================
⚠ CORRECTION (brief §3c implies it exists): the park is UNDER CONSTRUCTION — ground
broken Dec 10 2025, major work began Q1 2026, Phase 1 completion targeted FALL 2027.
And the county money is $500,000 TOTAL (two $250K Hudson County Open Space grants), not
$250K. Both synced to the brief.
WHO DECIDED: City of Hoboken (mayor + council), at the former Union Dry Dock site the
city acquired for $18.5M after a years-long fight (NY Waterway ferry-depot plan; a 2017
NJ Transit takeover attempt; litigation + eminent-domain ordinance Oct 2022).
  https://www.hobokennj.gov/news/city-of-hoboken-unveils-design-of-maritime-park-phase-i
  https://hudsoncountyview.com/city-of-hoboken-reaches-terms-to-acquire-union-dry-dock-from-ny-waterway-for-18-5m/
TIMELINE: ~decade of site fight → acquisition Dec 2022 → design (Dattner/SCAPE, then
Colliers; skatepark by Action Sports Design) → council unanimously approved final concept
Feb 7 2024 → bids due Nov 2025 → groundbreaking Dec 10 2025 → opening ~fall 2027.
THE SIGNATURE MOVE (why it's in our brief): 250+ survey respondents, 63% self-identified
skaters, plus an in-person skater design meeting — the features list traces directly to
that input. A cheap, replicable legitimacy tactic.
COST & FUNDING: Phase I ≈ $16.5M — $15M city bond + $1M State of NJ (Asm. John Allen) +
$500K county (2×$250K). Skatepark-only cost: UNVERIFIED (bundled). Full-park estimate
$74.5M, Phase 2 unfunded as of Mar 2026.
KEY PEOPLE: Mayor Ravi Bhalla ("crowning achievement"); Asm. John Allen ($1M state);
county executive/commissioners (open-space money); Fund for a Better Waterfront (ally
AND chief fiscal critic).
LESSONS: (1) the site fight, not the skatepark, was the battle; (2) ⚠ cost-transparency
blowback — the $74.5M estimate was withheld until after design approval, drawing
criticism: sequence cost disclosure honestly (relevant to our builder-quote step);
(3) same county funding vehicle as Berry Lane — the county open-space trust pattern.

5. ANDY KESSLER SKATEPARK (ex-Riverside), NYC — the youth-built original
====================================================================
WHO DECIDED: NYC Parks (owner/operator both eras); Riverside Park Conservancy supported
but did not control. ⚠ CORRECTIONS: original opened AUG 1996 (not 1995); original total
~$130K ($50K NPS "Innovations in Recreation" grant + $50K NYC Parks match + Riverside
Park Fund/City Parks Foundation contributions + donated materials); the youth program was
the Salvadori Institute (recruited the West Harlem/UWS teens) + Alternatives to Violence
Project (teamwork workshop) under Park Administrator Charles McKinney; teen builders were
paid $80/week. Rebuild: $2,864,000 city capital (Boro President + Council discretionary),
design W Architecture, skate construction California Skateparks; street plaza opened Aug
2020, bowl 2021; renamed for Andy Kessler (CB7 unanimous, Mar 2020; signage Oct 2021).
  https://www.pps.org/article/riversideskatepark
  https://www.nycgovparks.org/planning-and-building/capital-project-tracker/project/6733
  https://www.nycgovparks.org/parks/riverside-park/facilities/skateparks
STORY: early-90s — skater Andy Kessler proposes to Parks; teens design with him, build it
in ~5 weeks, summer 1996; first municipal skatepark in NYC. By 2012 the wood was rotting
and the park faced removal; volunteers under Ian Clarke (with Conservancy blessing) ran
permitted repairs + annual Skate Jams 2013–18, keeping it alive and visible — that
advocacy produced the $2.86M concrete rebuild AND the renaming.
2016 REDESIGN FIGHT: Parks' first concrete design dropped the vert progression; organized
skaters ("weak and exclusionary") forced a 6-to-11-ft pool bowl back in via CB7; then
Landmarks + Public Design Commission review (scenic-landmark park).
ACCESS TODAY: free; street plaza unsupervised/unfenced 6am–1am; the BOWL is seasonal
(Jun–Sep), limited hours, waiver + full pads required. Helmets/pads park-wide; the 1990s
$3 fee is long gone.
LESSONS: (1) wood construction nearly killed it — concrete is the survival decision;
(2) a dormant park was saved by users who kept it visibly loved (jams, volunteer repairs)
— stewardship is advocacy; (3) core users must be at the design table or the fight comes
later; (4) youth-built ownership (1996) is the deepest version of "the ownership is the
point" — and it started as one skater's proposal to a parks administrator.

6. BRENTWOOD, NY — ROBERTO CLEMENTE PARK SKATEPARK (the cost anchor, fully mapped)
====================================================================
WHO DECIDED: the TOWN OF ISLIP — town-owned park, every approval through the Islip Town
Board + Supervisor. State actors (DASNY, the AG) were funders/prosecutors, not deciders.
  https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2021/attorney-general-james-delivers-627000-brentwood-community-marred-waste-dumping
TIMELINE (~5 years; ⚠ conceived 2018, not 2019): Mar 2018 residents address the Town
Board; Supervisor Angie Carpenter + Parks meet them → Jan 2019 design kickoff → 2019
board approves initial contracts → 2020–21 stalls (pandemic, costs); Nov 2020 first
youth petition (Islip students, 500+) → May 2021 AG delivers $627K settlement money →
MAR 2022 construction resolution TABLED over a ~$452K inflation shortfall → May 28 2022
Brentwood Youth Advisory Board rally + Melgar/Ahmed petition (1,110 signatures) → within
weeks: board resolves to apply for/accept $450K DASNY → groundbreaking Jul 8 2022 →
OPENED MAR 24 2023.  https://patch.com/new-york/brentwood-central-islip/brentwood-skate-park-garners-needed-funding-after-outcry
⚠ PETITION NUANCE (corrects our correction): Melgar's petition pressured the TOWN to
allocate already-delivered settlement + grant money — the AG money had arrived a year
earlier. The accurate story: TWO youth petitions + a rally rescued a tabled project.
SIZE/SURFACE/BUILDER: ⚠ ~12,000 sq ft FOOTPRINT with ~7,000 sq ft actual SKATING surface
(deputy parks commissioner — most authoritative). Plaza-style concrete ("concrete" is
inferred from descriptions/photos, never stated — flag if load-bearing). Engineer:
Nelson + Pope (prime), with SITE DESIGN GROUP as skatepark design sub-consultant
(confirmed on N+P's own portfolio page). Construction contractor: UNVERIFIED (public
bid; never named in press).  https://nelsonpope.com/portfolio/roberto-clemente-park-pool-spray-park-and-skate-park/
COST & FUNDING — THE RECONCILED LEDGER (⚠ fixes a double-count in our dossier):
- As-built: $1.25M (News12 once said $1.2M — rounding).
- Press lists "$627K AG + $700K Ramos + $450K DASNY" as if separate ($1.78M ≠ $1.25M).
  Best-supported: **$700K = the $250K + $450K DASNY SAM grants, BOTH secured by Ramos**.
  True stack ≈ $627K AG toxic-dumping settlement + $700K Ramos/DASNY = $1.327M toward a
  $1.25M build, town absorbing the rest of the package. Exact ledger UNVERIFIED.
- ⚠ THE $4.5M RED HERRING, UPDATED: it's a MAR 24, 2026 Islip board resolution
  authorizing application for up to $4.5M DASNY SAM for "a skate park at Roberto
  Clemente Park" — a FUTURE ask (expansion? replacement? unclear), unrelated to the 2023
  build, and it does not approve construction. Anyone Googling "Brentwood skatepark
  $4.5M" will hit this — always cite $1.25M AS-BUILT (2023).
  https://liparks.com/articles/islip-roberto-clemente-park-skate-park-dasny-grant/_6874/
THE ORIGIN STORY (why it's OUR closest analogue): Aug 2013–Apr 2014, tens of thousands
of tons of construction debris illegally dumped in the park; closed May 2014 (asbestos,
heavy metals); ~39,000 tons removed; $6M town cleanup bond; felony convictions; reopened
2017. The AG's settlement recovery from 8 dumping companies then became a third of the
skatepark's funding — THE SCANDAL LITERALLY PAID FOR IT.
KEY PEOPLE: Supervisor Angie Carpenter (drove restoration; tabled then delivered);
Asm. Phil Ramos (both DASNY grants — our Lavine analog); AG Letitia James ($627K);
Alberto Melgar (HS senior, the 1,110-signature petition); Joshua Chan + Brentwood Youth
Advisory Board ("You constantly overlook Brentwood" — the equity line, to the
Supervisor's face); parks staff Wahlberg/Owens.
ACCESS TODAY: free, gated facility, skateboards/inline only (no BMX), gear
"recommended"; official islipny.gov policy UNVERIFIED (Cloudflare-blocked).
STATUS: open, well-regarded, centerpiece of an $11–13M park revitalization; May 2023
graffiti incident (cleaned within a week, prosecuted loudly); community group Uplift Our
Towns pushing further amenities (~$800K state, Jan 2025); the 2026 $4.5M application.
LESSONS: (1) approved ≠ built — a "Future Site" sign stood for years and became the
broken promise that fueled the youth movement; (2) an inflation shortfall nearly killed
it a SECOND time — rescued in weeks by youth pressure + a legislator's DASNY grant (the
exact Lavine play); (3) the equity narrative ("the overlooked side of town") was
leverage, wielded by the town's own youth board; (4) budget figures in press clippings
are traps — reconcile before citing.

7. LONG BEACH, NY — CITY SKATEPARK (the governance twin — our single best template)
====================================================================
⚠ CORRECTION: the "kids struck by a train" catalyst is NOT SUPPORTED — four targeted
searches found no such incident connected to the park. The real story: the old prefab
park (renovated 2007 as a memorial to Scott Reilly, a student who died of cancer in
2004) was damaged by Hurricane Sandy (2012), outdated, and badly sited by the sewage
plant. Corrected in dossier §8.
WHO DECIDED: the City of Long Beach itself — council-manager city (5-member council +
appointed City Manager). No county/town/state body owned the decision.
  https://liherald.com/stories/city-to-expedite-new-skate-park,61690
TIMELINE (~9 months vote→ribbon): new park entered the 5-yr capital plan (slated
FY2015-16) → Dec 2 2014 council UNANIMOUSLY approved a $5,000 Spohn Ranch design
contract AND accelerated construction a fiscal year under community pressure → two
public design meetings early 2015 (first design REJECTED by skaters; redrawn) → opened
Sept 6 2015 with a ribbon-cutting + skate jam (100+ skaters).
  https://www.liherald.com/stories/long-beach-opens-new-skate-park,71219
SIZE/SURFACE/BUILDER: poured concrete, ~7,000 sq ft skateable (the Herald's "15,000 sq
ft L-shape" is likely full site footprint — conflict noted). Spohn Ranch design-build.
Surf-themed, ~50/50 street/transition per skater request.
COST & FUNDING: **$300,000 TOTAL — city-bonded as part of the 5-year capital plan.** No
grants, no donations documented for the 2015 build. DPW's reusable defense: the park was
"less than 10%" of that year's >$3M road spend.
  PREDECESSOR STACK (2007, $99K — gold for us, same county): $37K city CDBG (via County
  Exec + Legislator), $25K NYS parks grant (via Assemblyman Weisenberg), $25K Nassau
  County, $12K raised by HIGH SCHOOL STUDENTS over 2 years ("Students Skate for Scott"),
  $7K surfcasting-tournament gift. https://www.liherald.com/stories/skate-park-reopens-in-long-beach,5315
KEY PEOPLE: City Manager Jack Schnirman (announced/framed the acceleration); DPW Comm'r
Jim LaCarrubba (ran the 5-firm RFP, chose the site, defended budget); Councilman Anthony
Eramo (visible elected champion — made "they redrew the design the skaters rejected" the
story); resident advocate Maggie Medrano; DPW secretary Christine Murphy (the inside-city-
hall staffer skaters first approached in 2005); Cliff Skudin (surf-community connector).
GOVERNANCE PATH: capital plan line → unanimous council vote (design contract + accelerate
+ bond authorization) → RFP procurement → public design meetings → build.
ACCESS TODAY: free, unfenced, no residency rule; city says 7am–10pm. Open and active;
no post-opening controversy found through 2026.
LESSONS: (1) an independent small city (pop ~33K) delivered a free concrete park for
$300K of ITS OWN BONDED MONEY, 9 months from vote to ribbon — the strongest possible
answer to "can a city like ours just do this?"; (2) letting skaters reject the first
design BUILT the buy-in; (3) objections were budget-priority, not anti-skatepark — and
the "<10% of road spend" comparison disarmed them; (4) the 2007 predecessor shows youth
fundraising + CDBG + county + state grants all working at small scale in NASSAU COUNTY.

8. ROCHESTER, NY — ROC CITY (the funding-stack playbook — and the long game won)
====================================================================
WHO DECIDED: City of Rochester (DES/Recreation operates); wrinkle — the land under the
Frederick Douglass–Susan B. Anthony bridge is owned by NYSDOT, adding permit complexity.
Phase 1's decisive money came administratively via the state's ROC the Riverway program.
  https://www.roccitymag.com/news-opinion/long-grind-how-skateboarders-shredded-a-13-year-road-to-rochesters-first-public-skatepark-12429290/
TIMELINE: Feb 2008 Friends of the Roc City Skatepark organizes (+$10K Tony Hawk Fdn
grant) → 2013 bridge-site feasibility ($50K study) → 2014 site pivot to Charles Carroll
Plaza, later scrapped → 2018 Cuomo's ROC the Riverway allocates $1M → groundbreaking Oct
9 2019 (date honors Akeer Matthews, a young skater who died in 2010) → PHASE 1 OPENS NOV
5 2020 (~12.75 years) → Jan 2023 $3M NYS DOS LWRP grant (CFA Rd 12) → council authorizes
Phase 2 Sept 2025 → Phase 2 groundbreaking Oct 16 2025 → opening OCT 2026, ahead of
schedule.  https://www.wxxinews.org/local-news/2026-07-06/finished-roc-city-skatepark-to-open-in-october-ahead-of-schedule
SIZE/SURFACE/BUILDER: concrete; Phase 1 ~14,300 sq ft skate area (a 26,000 figure is
likely site footprint); Phase 2 adds ~11,200 sq ft skate area + restrooms/event space
(~20K incl. site). Combined ~35,000 sq ft — claimed LARGEST concrete park in NY State.
Designer: Kanten Russell (pro-turned-designer; Stantec Ph1, New Line Ph2); builder:
Grindline, both phases.
COST & FUNDING: ⚠ CORRECTION — Phase 1 cost $1.5M (the $3.1M on the city page is the
multi-phase master plan). Ph1 stack: $1M NYS ROC the Riverway + $250K Tony Hawk Fdn/TSP
via BUILT TO PLAY (Ralph C. Wilson Jr. Fdn program — western-NY-only, NOT available to
us) + Sands Family Foundation (unrestricted startup + dollar-for-dollar match UP TO
$110K, construction-restricted) + RG&E, Xerox, individual donors, Friends fundraising.
Phase 2 ≈$4.68M: $3M LWRP (EPF) + ~$1.68M city capital.
  https://regionalcouncils.ny.gov/cfa/project/385892
  https://rochesterbeacon.com/2020/12/03/first-phase-of-downtown-skate-park-completed/
KEY PEOPLE: Alan Presutti (Friends ED/co-founder — the persistent face 2008–2026); Jim
Maddison (Friends founder/president — "promised his kids"); Aaron Costa (Krudco shop

friction; the residency wall generates durable regional resentment (…"a travesty" —

what our free/unfenced principle exists to avoid — while its Lenny Lamb origin shows the

endowment is the credible mechanism for our "maintenance answer," with the honest
caveat that even Ann Arbor's wasn't full on opening day; (4) nine years, sustained by
two leaders who never left.

13. SK8 CHARLESTON, SC — THE CEILING AND THE CAUTION (now with the post-opening story)
====================================================================
WHO DECIDED: Charleston County Park & Recreation Commission (CCPRC) — an independent,
fee-and-millage-funded county park agency; NOT the City of Charleston. The paid model is
agency-wide policy ("user fees to decrease reliance on tax dollars"), not a skatepark-
specific choice.  https://www.ccprc.com/1725/SK8-Charleston
TIMELINE: mid-2000s Ryan Cockrell (Pour It Now) begins lobbying → 2010 CCPRC allocates
$2M from reserves → slippage → concrete work 2015 → OPENED MAR 4 2017 (7 years funding→
ribbon; 10+ years advocacy).
SIZE/BUILD: 32,500 sq ft concrete (200-ft snake run, 11.5-ft pro bowl, street course,
lights, concessions, retail). Team Pain design-build. Total $4.8M (itemization beyond
the $2M reserve allocation UNVERIFIED; no state grant or philanthropy appears anywhere).
ACCESS MODEL (unchanged since opening): $3/day skate/BMX, $1 spectator, $40/yr pass,
signed waiver on file (parent for minors), mandatory helmet, staffed check-in, closed
when wet; Jan–Feb hours shortened. (⚠ our "county resident rate" detail is unsupported —
the fee is flat.)  https://www.ccprc.com/3172/Rules
POST-OPENING PERFORMANCE (the payoff of this profile):
- Year 1 (2017): >28,000 attendees. FY2022–23: 22,587 — ~20% BELOW year 1, six years on.
- At $3/day–$40/yr, gross admissions revenue is plausibly well under $100K/yr against a
  staffed 8–12hr/day operation (hard revenue-vs-cost figures UNVERIFIED).
- The fee model persists — no conversion to free access, but no evidence it "works"
  either; the county simply absorbs it as policy.
KEY PEOPLE: Ryan Cockrell / Pour It Now (10+ years of advocacy — and the group DRIFTED
AWAY before opening; why is unverified); Josh McFadden (CCPRC park manager).
NEARLY DIED / LESSONS: (1) opening-week press was literally headlined "…but some not
thrilled about its rules" — the gate/helmet/fee model alienated the core constituency on
day one, and some skaters kept riding the free DIY spot instead; (2) a world-class $4.8M
Team Pain build still drew only ~22–28K visits/yr when gated at $3 — versus Ann Arbor's
free, unfenced model at a quarter of the cost; (3) the founding advocates fell away
before the ribbon — a warning about what gated models do to community ownership. The
aspiration stands; the access model is the anti-pattern.
