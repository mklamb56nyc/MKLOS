01

It's in our DNA

An outdoor program here isn't a new idea. It's the oldest one we have.

In 1843, Sarah Porter made a radical choice: her students would study chemistry, physiology, botany, geology, and astronomy alongside traditional subjects. She required regular exercise, notable for the time, and encouraged sports like tennis, horseback riding, and baseball. The school even fielded its own team, the Tunxises, after the Tunxis people indigenous to Farmington.

Girls engaging with the physical and natural world was the founding vision. Bold by Nature picks that thread back up.

"Miss Porter's School educates young women to become informed, bold, resourceful and ethical global citizens."

Miss Porter's School mission
02

We already celebrate it

Mountain Day is the spark. Bold by Nature is the fire.

Every fall, without warning, classes stop and the whole school heads outdoors. Mountain Day is one of Porter's most cherished traditions, and it proves something useful: the appetite for a day outside, together, already runs deep here.

Bold by Nature doesn't compete with that day. It makes the feeling of it available all year, in ordinary weekends, with the same people you live and study with.

03

Our campus is made for it

We live on a nationally designated river.

In 2019, after a twelve-year community effort, Congress designated 61.7 miles of the lower Farmington River and Salmon Brook as a National Wild & Scenic River, and Farmington is one of the designated towns. The river at the edge of campus, the one our crews already row, carries the same federal designation as the country's great wild rivers.

And it's just the beginning of what's in reach.

Walking distance

Farmington River Trail & Hill-Stead

A riverside path and 152 acres of free estate trails, close enough for an outing with no bus at all.

20 minutes

Talcott Mountain

The Heublein Tower ridge walk, with fall views that reach Long Island Sound on a clear day.

35 minutes

Peoples State Forest

Eleven miles of blue-blazed trails on the Wild & Scenic West Branch. Snowshoe country in winter.

In town

Winding Trails

A 350-acre outdoor center in Farmington itself, home to Connecticut's only full-service cross-country ski center, rentals and lessons included.

Through the hills

The New England Trail

A National Scenic Trail along our traprock ridges, running all the way to the Sound at Guilford.

On the water

Paddling & partners

Flatwater canoeing from the Route 4 bridge, outfitters in Collinsville, and river-cleanup partners in the watershed association and land trust.

04

Our peer schools do it

The best schools in New England treat the outdoors as central.

The Dartmouth Outing Club, founded in 1909, is the oldest and largest collegiate outing club in the country; about a quarter of the student body belongs, and generations of students have chosen Dartmouth because of it. Williams, Colby, and Bowdoin each run their own version, and at each one the outing club is the largest organization on campus.

Closer to home, Exeter, Hotchkiss, Northfield Mount Hermon, and Holderness all have established outdoor programs. Porter's has the beginnings of one too — a student outdoors club already exists here, and the appetite it represents is real. Bold by Nature is a proposal to give that appetite a full program, built on our river, our hills, and our own traditions.

The precedent we love

Williams' Mountain Day

At Williams, the college's own Mountain Day is run by the outing club: bells ring, classes stop, and the club leads hikes for every ability, ending in cider and singing on Stony Ledge. The tradition belongs to the whole school; the club makes it happen.

How clubs begin

Colby's first trip

Colby's outdoor orientation started in 1975 with two students, one sympathetic staff member, and about twenty first-years. Fifty years on, every entering student goes.

What we'd borrow

The working formula

Student-led with an adult backstop, free gear, trips graded from mellow to ambitious, and no experience assumed. That combination is what makes these clubs the biggest thing on campus.

06

The bigger vision

Year one plants the seed.

The dream is bigger than a club calendar. It's a program woven into what it means to go to Porter's.

YR 1

A name people remember

A small founding circle, a few outings done well, and a culture that feels like Porter's from the first day.

YR 2–3

A New Girl outing

An outdoor welcome for incoming students. Even one night camping on the riverbank gives a whole class a shared memory.

YR 3–5

A signature expedition

A multi-day trip with its own traditions, maybe the Long Trail, maybe the coast of Maine, plus a gear library and trail journals that Old Girls hand down to New Girls.

YR 25

The reunion test

An Ancient at her 25th reunion says "remember that night on the river?" and every person in her class knows exactly what she means.

07

What we need to get started

The ask is small. The idea isn't.

  • A faculty advisor who shares the vision, ideally in science, sustainability, or athletics
  • Official club status through the student organizations process
  • A small budget for trailhead transportation and basic gear, $200–$500 to start
  • Five founding members ready to plan and lead the first outings this fall
  • Your enthusiasm. Come on one outing and see for yourself.

Let's get outside.
