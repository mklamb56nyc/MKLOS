# Bold by Nature — strategy

The positioning, name, and pillars for a student-run outdoor leadership club at
Miss Porter's School, founded and owned by Molly. Facts here are the verified
versions — sources and full detail in `research/`.

## The thesis

**Positioning matters as much as program design.** The club is framed as an
*extension of Porter's existing identity*, never as something new or imported:

- **It's a return to the founder's vision, not a departure.** In 1843 Sarah
  Porter had her students study chemistry, physiology, botany, geology, and
  astronomy alongside traditional subjects (confirmed verbatim on the school's
  Legacy page), required regular exercise, and encouraged sports — tennis,
  horseback riding, baseball per the school; skating and rowing per third-party
  histories. The school formed its own baseball team, the Tunxises — safe
  phrasing: "after the Tunxis people indigenous to Farmington."
- **The school's own language does the work.** The mission (verbatim): *"Miss
  Porter's School educates young women to become informed, bold, resourceful and
  ethical global citizens. We expect our graduates to shape a changing world."*
  And the Mission page's line about Sarah Porter's vision being *"radically brave
  in nature"* — the school already speaks in "bold" and "in nature."
- **Mountain Day is the leverage point** — a confirmed, beloved, surprise-announced
  fall tradition. Connect to it, don't compete with it: **"Mountain Day is the
  spark. Bold by Nature is the fire."** Proven precedent: Williams College's
  Mountain Day, where the outing club is the operational engine of the
  class-canceling tradition (`research/williams-colby-bowdoin.md`).
- **The campus is on a nationally designated river.** The Lower Farmington River
  was federally designated Wild & Scenic in March 2019 — through Farmington
  itself. Say "the riverside campus in Farmington"; don't quote acreage (sources
  conflict, 40 vs 55).
- **Molly doesn't need permission to start.** One outing this spring with friends,
  called Bold by Nature, creates the momentum that makes approval, advisor, and
  budget easier. (Colby's COOT began exactly this way: two students plus a
  sympathetic staffer, initial faculty rejection, ~20 students on the first trip —
  fifty years later it's mandatory for every first-year.)

## The name (locked)

**"Bold by Nature"** — chosen over dozens of candidates across four directions
(place-based/Tunxis, Porter's-vocabulary, mission-driven, direct nature names).
Why it won:

- Echoes the mission ("informed, **bold**, resourceful").
- References the school's own "radically brave **in nature**" language.
- Double meaning: bold *by their nature* AND bold *by way of nature* — neither
  overpowers the other.
- Avoids generic outdoors-club naming ("outing"/"hiking"/"wilderness" —
  explicitly rejected). "Sounds like something that belongs on a Porter's hoodie."
- Works as a standalone brand that can grow beyond a club.
- Confident without being aggressive — right for Porter's.

**Taglines (candidates, none finalized):**
- *"Bold by Nature: Outdoor Leadership at Miss Porter's School"* (used as the one-pager subtitle)
- *"Bold by Nature: Where Porter's Girls Find Their Edge"*
- *"Bold by Nature: Adventure, Stewardship & Leadership on the Farmington and Beyond"*

## The three pillars (locked)

The club's pillars map to themes Porter's genuinely emphasizes (note: don't
present "Leadership/Legacy/Sustainability" as the school's own formal framework —
that's a website nav, and its Leadership page is administrative):

1. **Sustainability & Stewardship** — rooted in the Farmington River ecosystem
   and the Connecticut landscape. Natural allies on campus: the student-run
   apiary, Earth Club, and the school's sustainability program (three-quarters
   of campus is natural space). Positioning note: Earth Club already exists, so
   Bold by Nature differentiates as adventure + leadership, partnering on
   stewardship rather than duplicating environmental activism.
2. **Leadership** — outdoor experiences ARE leadership development: leading a
   group through a rainstorm, navigation decisions, risk management — the same
   skills Porter's develops everywhere else.
3. **Legacy** — tradition-building and the Sarah Porter connection: an outdoor
   program as a return to the founder's radical vision.

## What great looks like (the analytic core)

From the benchmark research (full detail per program in `research/`), five
patterns separate great programs from merely good:

1. **A signature experience** that defines the program (DOC First-Year Trips,
   Holderness Out Back, Proctor Mountain Classroom) — something everyone knows
   about, even non-participants.
2. **Student ownership** — student-led is where leadership development actually
   happens. Refinement from research: the durable model everywhere is
   *student-led, professionally supported* (student board + adult backstop).
3. **Low barriers to entry** — engineered, not incidental: free gear, no-experience
   language, trips graded mild→strenuous, soft/arts/wellness options.
4. **Their own language & traditions** — internal vocabulary creates belonging
   and permanence (Trippees, COOT, Out Back, AWE).
5. **Connection to place** — *this* river, *these* hills, *this* landscape.

Cross-cutting findings: the top boarding-school tier (Thacher, CRMS, Proctor,
Athenian, Holderness) makes a significant wilderness experience **mandatory and
identity-defining**; the second differentiator is **progression** (beginners
developed into leaders over multiple years). Longevity comes from institutional
anchoring — facilities, staff, orientation mandates — not from continuous student
enthusiasm alone (Colby's club collapsed repeatedly before COOT anchored it).

**Peer-school proof points for the pitch:** Exeter, Hotchkiss, and NMH all have
verified outing clubs/outdoor programs. (Choate does not — dropped from the
peer list; its analog is the curricular Environmental Immersion Program.)
Closest structural analog to our starting point: Storm King's Outdoor Adventure —
single-teacher club (2021) → year-round by demand → points/ranks → multi-day
expeditions by 2025.

## The existing outdoors club (honesty rule)

A small student outdoors club **already exists** at Porter's (Mike, 2026-07-22;
largely inactive, nothing findable online — `research/miss-porters-school.md`
#12). This changes the pitch and the play:

- **Never claim or imply there is nothing.** The story is not "Porter's has no
  outdoor life" — it's "the appetite is already here; Bold by Nature gives it a
  program worthy of it." That framing is also simply stronger.
- **Chartering risk:** Student Life's easiest answer to a new-club application
  is "join the existing club." Molly needs a ready answer — and the honest one
  is that Bold by Nature is a different ambition (leadership program, signature
  traditions, year-round cadence), not a duplicate hiking sign-up sheet.
- **Its members are candidate allies, maybe co-founders.** Before Phase 4,
  Molly should find out who runs it and talk to them. The options run from
  partnering, to reviving/re-founding it under the Bold by Nature identity, to
  chartering separately with their blessing — her call once she knows the people.
  (Precedent either way: Colby's COOT grew out of the existing outing club;
  Storm King's program started as the new thing a coach built.)

## All-girls positioning

Differentiate intentionally from generic/co-ed models: lead with boldness,
power, and Sarah Porter's founding vision rather than importing a generic
"wilderness" identity. The school's athletics language (verbatim, safe to
quote): *"Porter's athletics begins and ends with girls. Our student athletes
learn to embrace challenges, set goals, take risks, and demonstrate their power
on and off the field."*

## Principles (don't drift)

- Position as an **extension of Porter's identity**, never something imported.
- **"Bold by Nature" lives inside the school's own language.** Keep it there.
- **Mountain Day is an asset to build on, not compete with.**
- Keep everything **executable by a motivated freshman**, not dependent on an adult.
- Signature experience + student ownership + low barriers + own language +
  connection to place = the five things that make it great.
- **Quote facts from `research/`, not from memory** — especially anything said
  to administrators.
